FIELD · grand strategy, nuclear proliferation & the use of force

Stop Iran's bomb by force — or by bargain?

On 22 June 2025 a flight of B-2s dropped bunker-busters on Fordow. A year later the guns are quiet under a 60-day clock. A dozen positions on what to do about Iran fold into four disagreements, and those into two questions: does force or diplomacy keep Tehran from a bomb, and can a regime like this one be deterred at all? The stakes — an Iranian weapon, a wider war, the Strait of Hormuz and the oil price, U.S. troops in missile range — are why the argument never cools. Below: the lineage, the positions and who holds them, and four cruxes, each with the strongest case both sides actually make.

I  the lineage

How we got to a B-2 over Fordow

Read top to bottom — events and the ideas that frame them, braided. A dot's halo marks where it leans: green for force and pressure, gold for restraint and diplomacy, lilac for the view that the framing is the trap.

1953
The coup against Mossadeghhistory · grievance
The CIA and Britain's MI6 help topple Iran's elected prime minister and restore the Shah, securing Western oil concessions. Iranians did not forget. Every later overture from Washington is read in Tehran through the memory of 1953 — the taproot of the distrust the whole standoff grows from.
1968 / 1970
The Non-Proliferation Treatylaw · the bargain
Iran signs as an original party. The NPT's deal: have-nots forgo weapons in exchange for civilian nuclear technology and a promise of disarmament from the haves. Article IV's "inalienable right" to enrichment and the line between civilian and weapons work become the legal ground every side still fights on.
1979
Revolution & the hostage crisishistory · the break
The Shah falls; an Islamic Republic rises on "Death to America." Students seize the U.S. embassy and hold 52 Americans for 444 days. Diplomatic relations snap and never resume. For American hawks this is the founding fact: a state born in the taking of hostages is not a normal negotiating partner.
1981
Osirakmilitary · the template
Israeli jets flatten Iraq's French-built reactor before it can go hot — the "Begin Doctrine," that no hostile state in the region gets a weapon. Condemned at the time, later quietly credited with keeping Saddam non-nuclear. The preventive-strike model that Fordow's planners inherited.
1981 → 2012
Kenneth Waltztheory · deterrence
The dean of realist IR argues that nuclear weapons induce caution, not recklessness — the spread of the bomb has tracked with the absence of great-power war. In "Why Iran Should Get the Bomb" (Foreign Affairs, 2012) he takes it to the edge: a nuclear Iran would be deterrable, even stabilizing, and the crisis is manufactured by people who don't trust deterrence to do what it has always done.
2002
Natanz revealed · "Axis of Evil"intelligence · alarm
An exile group exposes a secret enrichment plant at Natanz; Bush names Iran in the "Axis of Evil." The covert program is now public, and the clock everyone has argued about since — how close is Tehran to a weapon? — starts ticking in the open.
2003
The Iraq Warhistory · the warning
The U.S. invades on WMD intelligence that turns out to be wrong, and a quick "regime change" becomes a decade of occupation. Restraint thinkers cite it as the cautionary tale: certainty about an adversary's weapons program and confidence that toppling a government will go well are exactly the beliefs that produced a catastrophe.
2015
The JCPOAdiplomacy · the deal
Iran and six powers sign the nuclear deal: enrichment capped near 3.7%, centrifuges mothballed, the stockpile shipped out, and the most intrusive IAEA inspections ever written, traded for sanctions relief. The high-water mark of the bargain — a verified ceiling in place of a strike.
2018 → 2020
Withdrawal · maximum pressure · Soleimanipolicy · the turn
Trump quits the JCPOA and reimposes sanctions under "maximum pressure," betting that economic pain forces a better deal. Iran responds by enriching past every cap — eventually to 60%. In January 2020 a U.S. drone kills Qassem Soleimani, Iran's most powerful general, in Baghdad. The pressure track is now the policy.
Jun 2025 → Feb 2026
Fordow, then the 2026 warmilitary · the strikes
In the "12-Day War" with Israel, U.S. B-2s hit Fordow, Natanz, and Isfahan on 22 June 2025 — fourteen GBU-57 bunker-busters and thirty submarine-launched Tomahawks. On 28 February 2026 a far larger U.S.–Israeli campaign opens the "2026 Iran war," roughly 900 strikes in twelve hours; Supreme Leader Khamenei is killed. Force, at full scale.
Apr → Jun 2026
Ceasefires & the MOUdiplomacy · the pause
A short Pakistan-mediated truce in April; then on 17 June 2026 Trump and President Pezeshkian sign a 14-point memorandum of understanding — a 60-day ceasefire, the Strait reopened, a road toward a final deal. It settles the shooting, not the substance. The hinge the war bent the policy toward.
Into the present

As of late June 2026 the guns are quiet on a 60-day clock, with no agreement yet on enrichment rights, the highly-enriched-uranium stockpile, sanctions relief and frozen assets, or Hezbollah and Lebanon. Whether this ends in a durable deal, an Iranian bomb, or a return to war is the open question — and it is open today.

II  the positions

Who holds what — and where it sits on the axis

Two families: what to do with the tools of statecraft, and what counts as winning. A card's left edge is its lean; the tag is rough policy weight today, not a scorecard of who's right.

On the use of force coerce or hold back

◀ force Preventive counter-proliferation strikes dominant in 2025–26 US/Israel policy
Israeli leadership · U.S. hawks · the Begin Doctrine
A nuclear-armed Iran is intolerable, and force can deny it or push it back years. When a hostile state nears the threshold, you destroy the capability rather than learn to live with the weapon.
The logic that produced Osirak in 1981 and Fordow in 2025 — act before the program is finished, not after.
Washington Institute analysis
◀ force Maximum pressure the 2018–24 sanctions doctrine
Trump-era Treasury · hawkish advisers
Crippling sanctions plus a credible threat of force compel Tehran to capitulate on better terms than any deal would give. Squeeze hard enough and the regime chooses survival over enrichment.
Critics and supporters agree it raised the pressure; they split on whether it bought leverage or just more centrifuges.
▶ restraint Diplomatic restraint realist/progressive minority, growing
Quincy Institute · Cato · Trita Parsi
Strikes backfire — they harden Iran's resolve and scatter the program without ending it. A negotiated ceiling, verified by inspectors, is the only path that actually holds the bomb down for long.
Not pacifism: the claim is that force is the less effective tool against a problem that lives in knowledge and intent.
Cato
▶ restraint Deterrence / containment the academic realist baseline
Kenneth Waltz · structural realists
Even a near-nuclear or nuclear Iran can be deterred the way every other adversary has been. The bomb makes states cautious; containment and the threat of overwhelming retaliation are cheaper and surer than war.
The most provocative version says a deterred nuclear Iran beats a perpetual war to prevent one.
Quincy Institute

On the endgame what victory means

◀ force Regime change / collapse aspirational, contested
hardline hawks · diaspora opposition
The problem isn't the centrifuges, it's the Islamic Republic. Topple it — or back the Iranian people until it falls — and the nuclear question, the proxies, and the hostility all go with it.
The Iraq and Libya record is the standing objection: collapse is easy to start and hard to aim.
▶ restraint Negotiated nuclear deal the current track
JCPOA architects · the 2026 MOU negotiators
Verified limits on enrichment in exchange for sanctions relief. The 2015 model, picked back up: a ceiling you can inspect beats a program you can only bomb.
Where the 60-day MOU is pointed — down to the line about downgrading uranium from weapons-grade to reactor-grade.
◀ force "The strikes worked" the administration's read
strike planners · Pentagon leadership
Fordow's enrichment halls are wrecked and the program is set back years. Pressure and bombs, not talk, are what finally brought Tehran to sign.
Leans on the later Pentagon estimate of a one-to-two-year setback over the early intelligence read.
▶ restraint "The strikes backfired" the skeptics' read
arms-control analysts · restraint scholars
The bombs delayed the program by months, not years; the enriched-uranium stockpile was moved out beforehand, and the lesson Tehran drew is that a bomb is its only real insurance.
Rests on the preliminary, low-confidence DIA assessment — itself contested by later estimates.
the hinge ↕
Coercive diplomacy

Pressure and the credible threat of force, aimed not at toppling the regime but at moving it to a deal it would otherwise refuse. Both poles collapse toward this in practice: the hawk who wants the strikes to end in an agreement and the restrainer who admits that talks went nowhere until Iran felt real cost are standing closer than either likes to say. It is roughly what the June 2026 MOU is — a ceasefire reached at gunpoint, now trying to become something signed. The pragmatic center the war actually pushed the policy toward, and the place most of the argument quietly lands once the slogans are set down.

Tags are rough policy weight — how much pull a position has in the debate right now — not a verdict. "Traction" here means how many serious people are on it, not who is correct; the two keep separate books.
III  the cruxes

Four fair fights, each carrying part of the load

The positions are bundles of answers; these are the questions that, answered one way or the other, split them. Both panels give the side's best case, in its own voice.

Crux 01 — the strikemilitary · proliferation

Does military force stop an Iranian bomb, or speed it up?

Fordow has been hit. The question is what the bombing bought — years of delay, or a regime now convinced it needs the weapon.

▸ force is the only language left
Strike the program
Twenty years of diplomacy failed to stop the centrifuges; bombs did the thing talk never could, and bought years.

Diplomacy got its run. From 2003 to 2025, talks and inspections and three rounds of sanctions could not keep Iran below the threshold — it enriched to 60%, a short sprint from weapons-grade, while everyone negotiated. Credible force is the one thing the regime has ever actually responded to: the strikes wrecked enrichment halls that took a decade to build, and Tehran signed a ceasefire weeks later, not because it was persuaded but because it was hurt. Israel has kept hostile programs back for forty years by hitting them — Osirak, Syria's reactor in 2007, now Fordow. "Mowing the grass" isn't a permanent fix; it's what you do when the permanent fixes have all been tried and failed. You buy time and you use it.

▸ you can't bomb away knowledge
Strikes harden the resolve
The setback is months, the stockpile was already moved, and the lesson Tehran takes is that only a bomb guarantees survival.

The Defense Intelligence Agency's first read put the delay in months, and Iran had trucked much of its enriched uranium out of Fordow before the bombers arrived. Centrifuge cascades can be rebuilt; the metallurgy and the physics live in the heads of people the strikes didn't kill. And look at what bombing teaches. Qaddafi gave up his weapons program in 2003 and was dead in a ditch eight years later; the regimes that kept their programs — North Korea — are the ones nobody invades. Hit Iran and you confirm, in the only currency the hardliners trust, that a deterrent is the difference between Saddam's fate and Kim's. The surest way to make Tehran build the thing you fear is to show it that not having it gets you Fordow.

the crux Is proliferation a physical problem — destroy the centrifuges and you've bought time — or a motivational one, where the strike just hardened the resolve to acquire? Almost everything about force follows from which.
what would move them

The force side concedes if the strikes are shown to have only delayed the program by months while hardening Iran's resolve to build a weapon.

The restraint side concedes if force is shown to have set the program back years and won concessions Iran would otherwise have refused.

Crux 02 — the regime readpsychology of the adversary · crossways

Is Iran's leadership a rational survival-seeker, or an implacable ideological actor?

Before you argue about strikes or deals, you've already decided what kind of adversary you're dealing with. Most of the fight is downstream of that.

▸ an ideological regime, not a normal state
Implacable by design
A theocracy that funds Hezbollah and Hamas and chants "Death to America" runs on doctrine, not cost-benefit, and can't be handed a deterrent.

This is a government that has organized its foreign policy around the destruction of Israel and the expulsion of the United States from the region for four decades, and built a ring of proxies — Hezbollah, Hamas, the Houthis, the Iraqi militias — to wage that fight by other hands. It calls Israel a "one-bomb country," language no status-quo power uses. Deterrence assumes an adversary who prizes its own survival above all; an apocalyptic ideology that valorizes martyrdom and exports revolution does not give you that assumption for free. You cannot run a containment policy on the hope that the people who built this system will, at the last, behave like cautious bureaucrats. Hand that regime a bomb and you are betting the region on a read of its soul.

▸ forty years of acting to survive
A state, not a death cult
The regime has always chosen its own preservation, complied under the JCPOA, and deterrence held against worse men than the Ayatollah.

Watch what Tehran does, not what it chants. It ended its war with Iraq when the cost grew unbearable. It froze its weaponization work in 2003 when exposure looked dangerous. It signed the JCPOA and the IAEA certified, repeatedly, that it complied. It calibrated its retaliation for Soleimani to wound American pride without killing Americans, precisely to avoid the war that would end the regime. That is not a martyrdom machine; that is a state reading the balance of risk. Deterrence held against Stalin, who starved millions, and against Mao, who spoke of surviving nuclear war — men far more frightening than a clerical establishment whose overriding project is staying in power. Treat Iran as the survival-seeking state its behavior shows it to be, and the apocalyptic framing looks like a reason for war in search of a justification.

the crux Rational survival-seeker or ideological outlier? Nearly every other disagreement on this map is downstream of this single read of the regime. ↳ this crux runs crossways to the others — it's the hidden second axis, the one the force-vs-diplomacy line is plotted against.
what would move them

The force side concedes if Iran's leadership behaves as a cost-sensitive survival-seeker that honors a verifiable deal over time.

The restraint side concedes if Iran races for a weapon despite an open off-ramp, or spends sanctions relief mainly on proxies and rebuilding the program.

Crux 03 — the dealdiplomacy · verification

Can any agreement with Tehran actually hold?

The 2026 MOU points toward a final deal. Whether a deal is an instrument that binds Iran or a document that buys it time is the thing under dispute.

▸ deals with Iran expire or get gamed
A ceiling on a timer
The JCPOA's sunset clauses just postponed the problem, inspections can be stalled, and a regime that cheated at the margins will again.

Read the fine print of the last deal. Its key limits were written to lapse — enrichment caps sunsetting on a calendar, after which Iran could legally expand toward a weapon with the agreement still nominally in force. That is not a solution; it is a delay with a signing ceremony. And verification has limits inspectors will admit in private: Iran stonewalled the IAEA on past weaponization work, sanitized the Turquzabad site before inspectors got in, and kept an entire archive of its weapons program that Israeli intelligence later hauled out of a Tehran warehouse. You cannot inspect what you are not shown. A deal hands the regime sanctions relief and legitimacy now, in exchange for promises that expire and access it can throttle. The grass grows back faster on paper than in a bombed-out tunnel.

▸ the deal worked until we left it
A verified cap beats no cap
Under the JCPOA the program was boxed and inspected; it was the 2018 U.S. withdrawal that freed Iran to enrich to 60%.

The counterfactual is sitting right there in the timeline. From 2016 to 2018, with the JCPOA in force, Iran's enrichment was capped near 3.7%, its centrifuges were mothballed, its stockpile shipped abroad, and IAEA cameras watched the centrifuge workshops. Inspectors certified compliance again and again. Then the United States walked out of a deal Iran was keeping, reimposed sanctions, and within two years Tehran was enriching to 60% with the cameras switched off. The lesson is not that agreements with Iran fail; it is that this one was working and was abandoned by the side that wasn't even cheating. A deal with intrusive verification gives you eyes inside the program and a tripwire if it moves. No deal gives you neither, and the only alternative left is to bomb the same tunnels every few years.

the crux Are agreements with Iran enforceable instruments that bind behavior, or theater that buys the regime time and cover? The case for the diplomatic track stands or falls here.
what would move them

The force side concedes if intrusive inspections verifiably cap enrichment for years, as they did before the 2018 withdrawal.

The restraint side concedes if Iran is caught materially cheating under inspections, or sunset clauses are shown to merely postpone breakout.

Crux 04 — the costconstitution · economics

Who decides, and who pays?

The strikes were ordered without a vote. The bill — a closed Strait, an oil shock, troops in range — comes to everyone.

▸ a president must be able to act
Speed against an existential threat
Against a proliferation window measured in weeks, the commander-in-chief has to move fast — and a limited strike is not a land war.

A nuclear breakout doesn't wait for a floor debate. If Fordow is weeks from weapons-grade and a narrow window opens to set it back, a president who has to assemble 218 House votes first will watch the window close. The Constitution makes one person commander-in-chief precisely so the country can act when speed is the whole game; presidents from both parties have ordered strikes — Libya, Kosovo, Syria — without a declaration of war, and the Republic stands. These were limited operations against specific sites, not an occupation: bombers in and out in a night, no boots committed to hold ground. Treating every use of force as if it were the invasion of Iraq misreads what happened. The alternative to decisive action isn't a cleaner process; it's an Iranian bomb that no later vote can undo.

▸ war by one man breaks the design
Diffuse costs, concentrated power
Starting a war without Congress inverts the constitutional order, and the costs land on everyone while the decision sits with one.

The Framers gave Congress the power to declare war for a reason they stated plainly: the person who can order the killing should not be the same person who decides whether killing begins. "Limited" is the word every war uses on day one. The strikes on Fordow risked, and the 2026 war delivered, exactly the escalation skeptics warned of — a closed Strait of Hormuz, through which a fifth of the world's oil moves; a price shock that taxes every household on earth; missiles aimed at the tens of thousands of American troops within Iran's reach; proxies activated from Lebanon to Yemen. Those costs are spread across a planet that got no say. The decision sat with one man at dinner. A system that lets a single official commit the country to a war whose bill everyone else pays has stopped being the one the Constitution describes.

the crux Do diffuse costs and constitutional limits outweigh decisive executive action against a proliferating adversary? It's the one crux that's about us, not Tehran.
what would move them

The force side concedes if the strikes trigger the wider war, oil shock, or proxy escalation critics warned of, at a cost beyond the delay they bought.

The restraint side concedes if decisive executive action is shown to have averted a near-term bomb that deliberation would have allowed, with manageable blowback.

IV  the payoff

Four cruxes, two crossed questions underneath

One axis is the loud one: force or diplomacy. But cut sideways and a quieter axis appears — is Iran the kind of state you can deter, or an actor that won't be? The strike-vs-deal argument is being plotted against that second question all along. Plot both and the field stops looking like one fight. It looks like people standing on different squares, each answering a question the other side never asked.

Crux 01 · the strike
Does force stop a bomb

The horizontal axis, asked of the bombs. Physical setback, or a program that just got more motivated.

Crux 02 · the regime read
Is Iran deterrable

The vertical axis — the hidden one. Survival-seeking state, or ideological outlier; the read the rest hangs on.

Crux 03 · the deal
Can a deal hold

The horizontal axis again, aimed at diplomacy. Enforceable instrument, or theater that buys time.

Crux 04 · the cost
Who decides and pays

The horizontal axis brought home. Decisive executive action, or diffuse costs no one voted for.

Most arguments about Iran are really arguments about the regime — the vertical axis. Work out whether you think Tehran can be deterred like any state or is an actor that won't be, and you'll usually find your opponent disagreeing with you about that, and only seeming to disagree about the bombs.

Each steelman is compressed to its strongest form — the hawk's "force is the only language Tehran respects" and the restrainer's "you can't bomb away knowledge" both run to books and briefings, and a panel here is a doorway, not the room. Mapping the best case for both sides is not endorsing either; the point is to find which question you actually disagree on, not to settle it. This is a live conflict — written in late June 2026, with a 60-day ceasefire running and nothing final signed — so specifics will age fast; check the facts links before relying on any of it.
