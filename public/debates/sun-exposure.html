FIELD · dermatology, photobiology & public health

Sunlight: a carcinogen to block, or a nutrient we're starved of?

A century of advice that flipped — from prescribing the sun to fleeing it, and now back again. A dozen positions on whether to shade your skin or seek the light fold into four disagreements, and those into two questions: whether you weigh the whole body or only the skin, and whether the thing that heals is a molecule a pill can copy or a dose only the sun delivers. What rides on it: what you put on a child at the beach, whether a pale office worker is deficient or just prudent, and whose expertise gets to write the label. Below — the lineage, the positions and who holds them, and four cruxes, each with the strongest case both sides actually make.

I  the lineage

From cure, to carcinogen, and partway back

Read top to bottom. A dot's halo marks where the work landed — green for "the sun harms, shade it," gold for "the sun heals, we're starved of it," lilac for the reading that the dose and the skin were the missing variable all along.

1903
Niels Finsenphototherapy · Nobel Prize
A Danish physician wins the Nobel for treating lupus vulgaris — a disfiguring skin tuberculosis — with concentrated light. The sun and its lamps are cutting-edge medicine. For the first half of the century, a tan means health, and doctors send the sick outdoors.
1900s–30s
Auguste Rollierheliotherapy · alpine clinics
In the Swiss Alps, Rollier runs sun clinics where tuberculosis patients are laid out on balconies to heal in graduated doses of mountain sunlight. "Heliotherapy" is mainstream, and its central rule — build the dose slowly, never burn — is a lesson the field will spend a century forgetting and relearning.
1919–22
Huldschinsky · vitamin Dnutrition · rickets
Kurt Huldschinsky cures rachitic children with ultraviolet lamps; researchers soon isolate vitamin D and connect the dots — sunlight on skin makes the vitamin that builds bone. Rickets, a scourge of sunless industrial cities, becomes preventable with light. The sun is, quite literally, nutrition.
1956
UV → skin cancerepidemiology · the turn
The evidence tying ultraviolet light to skin cancer hardens. Melanoma and the far commoner keratinocyte cancers get pinned on sun exposure, especially in fair skin. The verdict begins to invert: the thing that made bone also breaks DNA.
1981
Slip, Slop, Slappublic health · the campaign
Australia — the melanoma capital of the world — launches Sid the Seagull and the slogan that defines the era: slip on a shirt, slop on sunscreen, slap on a hat. Photoprotection becomes a public-health movement. Over the next decades, "avoid the midday sun, cover up, wear SPF daily" hardens into standard advice across the fair-skinned world.
2004
Michael Holickendocrinology · the dissent
The UV Advantage argues that sensible, non-burning sun exposure is healthy and that blanket avoidance is driving widespread vitamin D deficiency. Holick is asked to step down as chair of dermatology at his university; he keeps his endocrinology post. The clash makes the split institutional — and personal.
2009
IARC · Group 1carcinogen classification
The WHO's cancer agency places UV-emitting tanning beds in Group 1 — the same "carcinogenic to humans" tier as tobacco and asbestos. Solar radiation was already there. On the harm side, the science is now about as settled as cancer epidemiology gets.
2014
Richard Wellerdermatology · nitric oxide
An Edinburgh dermatologist shows that sunlight on skin releases nitric oxide, dilating blood vessels and lowering blood pressure — a benefit that has nothing to do with vitamin D. Since cardiovascular disease kills orders of magnitude more people than melanoma, he argues the ledger has been kept with only one column.
2016
Lindqvist · Swedish cohortepidemiology · mortality
Following ~29,000 Swedish women for twenty years, Pelle Lindqvist reports that sun avoiders die earlier — and that the size of the effect is comparable to smoking. Nonsmokers who avoided the sun had life expectancy like smokers who sought it. The finding reframes sun exposure as an all-cause-mortality question, not just a skin one.
2019–22
VITAL · D-HealthRCTs · the vitamin fails the test
Large randomized trials of vitamin D supplements — VITAL in the US, D-Health in Australia — find little effect on cancer, heart disease, or death. For the harm camp this deflates the vitamin-D case; for the benefit camp it sharpens it: if the pill doesn't work but the sunlight does, the benefit was never really about the vitamin.
2020s
Circadian light · the online reopeningchronobiology · culture
Morning-light and circadian arguments go mainstream through popular science; a "sunscreen is toxic, go outside" backlash surges on social media, and dermatologists push back hard against it. The mainstream position holds — burning and tanning beds are still out — but the framing shifts toward dose, skin type, and what you're trading off, not a single rule for everyone.
Into the present

A century in, the two hardest facts sit side by side and both are true: UV causes skin cancer, and sun avoidance tracks with dying younger. The argument has migrated from "sun: good or bad?" to a narrower set — how much, on which skin, at which latitude, weighed against what — and who gets to set the number.

II  the positions

Who holds what — and where it sits on the axis

Two families of position: claims about what sunlight does to the skin, and claims about what it does to the rest of the body. A card's left edge is its lean; the tag is rough sociology, not a scorecard.

On the skin cancer & aging

◀ shade it Photoprotection consensus the standing guideline
AAD · WHO/IARC · Cancer Council Australia
UV is a Group 1 carcinogen with no safe tanning dose. Seek shade, cover up, wear broad-spectrum sunscreen daily, and never burn. Get vitamin D from diet or a pill, not from courting DNA damage.
The advice most institutions issue; its weakest spot is treating every body and latitude as one fair-skinned Australian at noon.
AAD: sunscreen FAQs
◀ shade it Melanoma-first dermatology clinical mainstream
practicing dermatologists · skin-cancer surgeons
Melanoma can kill a young, otherwise healthy person, and the common keratinocyte cancers cost a fortune in surgeries and scars. The prudent default is prevention, and intermittent burning in fair skin is the worst pattern of all.
Anchored in what a clinic actually sees; less moved by population-level mortality abstractions.
Melanoma
◀ shade it Photoaging & cosmetic case broad, consumer-facing
cosmetic dermatology · skincare science
Most of what people call "aging" skin — wrinkles, spots, leathering — is cumulative UV, not time. Daily sunscreen is the single best-evidenced anti-aging step there is.
On strong ground for the skin's appearance; says nothing about the rest of the body.
Photoaging

On the whole body the systemic case

▶ seek it Vitamin D advocates large following, contested in trials
Michael Holick · endocrinology dissenters
Sensible, non-burning sun is the natural way to make vitamin D, and blanket avoidance has left much of the indoor world deficient. Skin makes far more, far faster, than most people eat.
Reframes sun as nutrition; dented by supplement trials that failed to show the promised benefits.
Michael F. Holick
▶ seek it Nitric-oxide / cardiovascular newer, gaining traction
Richard Weller · photobiology labs
Sunlight on skin releases nitric oxide and lowers blood pressure — independent of vitamin D. Since heart disease and stroke dwarf melanoma as killers, even a small blood-pressure benefit could outweigh the skin-cancer cost at the population level.
Its strength is a mechanism a pill can't copy; still early, and short of long-term outcome trials.
Nitric oxide
▶ seek it Sun-avoidance-as-risk influential single cohort
Pelle Lindqvist · the Swedish women's study
Followed for two decades, women who avoided the sun died earlier across major causes — an effect on the order of smoking. Total avoidance isn't safety; it's a risk factor with a body count.
The most striking number in the debate — and the most exposed to confounding by who chooses the sun.
Lindqvist et al., 2016
▶ seek it Circadian / light-environment popular, mechanistically grounded
chronobiology · popular science
Bright daytime light — especially in the morning — sets the circadian clock, improving sleep, mood, and metabolic health. The relevant deficiency is spending life indoors under weak artificial light, not UV as such.
Sidesteps the cancer trade-off by leaning on visible light and timing rather than UV dose.
Circadian rhythm
the hinge ↕
It's the dose and the skin, not all-or-nothing

The reconciling read says both slogans are too coarse to be true. "The sun is poison" and "the sun is medicine" both pretend there's one answer for a body, when the biology turns on variables the poles average away. It's dose: a regular, non-burning amount behaves nothing like an intermittent sunburn, and it's the burn — the acute, blistering hit on skin that rarely sees light — that carries most of the melanoma risk. It's skin: a fair Scot and a dark-skinned Nigerian have wildly different cancer risk and different vitamin-D economics from the same photons; one rule cannot fit both. It's latitude and season: winter sun in Stockholm can't make vitamin D at all, while equatorial noon is a different exposure entirely. Rollier knew the rule in 1910 — build the dose slowly, never burn. The century-long fight is, in part, the cost of collapsing that gradient into a single yes-or-no.

Tags are rough sociology — how the specialties and the literature shake out — not a verdict on who's right. Dermatology owns the skin endpoint; the systemic case is argued mostly from outside it. A position can be institutional and incomplete, or marginal and onto something.
III  the cruxes

Four fair fights, each carrying part of the load

The positions are bundles of answers; these are the questions that, answered one way or the other, split them. Both panels give the side's best case, in its own voice.

Crux 01 — the ledgerepidemiology

Add up every effect on the whole body — does the sun end up net-harmful or net-helpful?

Skin cancer is a real cost. So, apparently, is avoidance. When you total the columns — cancer against blood pressure, mood, mortality — which way does the sum fall?

▸ the harm is proven, the benefit is soft
Net harm — bank the certain avoided cancer
Skin cancer from UV is about as well-established as causation gets; the systemic benefits are mostly observational and unproven in trials. You don't trade a certainty for a maybe.

Weigh the evidence by how hard it is to fake. UV causing skin cancer sits on molecular mechanism, dose-response, animal models, and a Group 1 classification — the whole apparatus. The benefit side rests on one striking cohort and a blood-pressure mechanism that hasn't yet produced an outcomes trial. When a hazard is certain and a benefit is speculative, the prudent move is to prevent the certain harm — especially when the benefit has a safe substitute. Want the vitamin? Take it as a pill. Want the mortality edge the sun-seekers seem to have? It's far more likely their money, their exercise, and their health that you're seeing than their photons. Don't court a proven carcinogen chasing an effect you can get another way or that may not be real.

▸ the ledger was kept with one column
Net benefit — the skin is a rounding error on the body
Melanoma is real but rare; the systemic upsides touch the diseases that actually kill. Optimize only the skin and you can win the small fight while losing the large one.

Look at what each side is counting. Dermatology counts skin, and on skin it's right. But cardiovascular disease kills orders of magnitude more people than melanoma, and if sunlight nudges blood pressure down through nitric oxide, that lever moves the biggest killer there is. The Swedish cohort didn't measure a tan — it measured dying, across all causes, and the avoiders lost years on a scale we compare to smoking. A public-health machine built entirely around the skin endpoint will happily trade a large, diffuse mortality benefit for a small, visible cancer reduction and call it a win, because it never had the whole body on its books. Count what kills, not what's easiest to photograph, and the sum flips.

the crux Do you total the ledger over the skin or over the whole body? The harm side trusts the certain, narrow harm; the benefit side bets on the diffuse, systemic upside — and half the disagreement is just which column you're allowed to count.
what would move them

The shade-it side concedes if randomized or quasi-experimental trials show moderate sun exposure lowers all-cause mortality even after wealth, exercise, and outdoor lifestyle are fully controlled for.

The seek-it side concedes if the mortality gap in sun-seekers dissolves once socioeconomic and activity confounders are removed, leaving skin cancer as the only robust net effect.

Crux 02 — the mechanismphotobiology

Whatever good the sun does — is it the vitamin D, which a pill can copy?

If the benefit is vitamin D, avoidance costs nothing: swallow the vitamin. If it's something else sunlight does, the pill is beside the point.

▸ it was the vitamin, and the pill failed
Supplement it and stay out of the sun
The one clear pathway from sun to health was vitamin D — and when it was tested as a pill in big trials, it didn't deliver. So either the vitamin was oversold, or you can just take it. Either way, no UV required.

For years the benefit case leaned on vitamin D, so we tested it the honest way: give thousands of people the vitamin, blinded, for years. VITAL and D-Health came back mostly null on cancer, heart disease, and death. Read that straight. If vitamin D drives the benefit, a pill supplies it without a single photon — sun avoidance costs you nothing you can't replace at the pharmacy. And if the pill doesn't work, then the low vitamin D seen in sick people was probably a marker of poor health, not its cause — sick, sedentary, indoor people have low D because they're sick, not sick because their D is low. The other proposed pathways are interesting lab findings, not established medicine. Don't accept real DNA damage to chase mechanisms that haven't earned a recommendation.

▸ the vitamin was the marker, not the mechanism
The pill was always the wrong test
That the supplement failed is our point. Sunlight does things a capsule can't — release nitric oxide, entrain the circadian clock — so a null pill trial refutes the vitamin, not the sun.

Everyone ran the same experiment and drew opposite lessons. We agree the pill mostly failed. But blood vitamin D was always partly a readout of how much time you spend outdoors, moving, in the light — a proxy for a whole exposure. Isolate one molecule, put it in a capsule, and you've stripped away everything else the sun was doing: the nitric oxide that drops blood pressure, the morning light that sets your clock and your sleep, the daytime brightness tied to mood and metabolism. None of that is in the pill, so of course the pill underperforms the sunshine. That's not evidence the sun is inert — it's evidence we were measuring the wrong thing and bottling the wrong part. You can't supplement your way to a photon's full effect.

the crux Is sunlight's benefit reducible to vitamin D — and therefore replaceable by a pill — or does it run through pathways (nitric oxide, circadian light) a capsule can't reproduce? The same null supplement trials read as deflating or sharpening depending on your answer.
what would move them

The shade-it side concedes if a non-vitamin-D pathway — nitric oxide, circadian light — is shown in trials to improve hard outcomes that supplements and indoor light can't reproduce.

The seek-it side concedes if the systemic benefits turn out to be fully captured by vitamin D status (or another suppliable factor), making a pill a complete substitute for the sun.

Crux 03 — the confoundercausal inference

Sun-seekers live longer — because of the sun, or because of who seeks it?

The headline number is observational. People who get more sun are also, on average, richer, thinner, more active, on holiday. Is the sun doing the work, or the lifestyle around it?

▸ the signal survives the controls
A dose-response this clean isn't just lifestyle
The cohorts adjust for the obvious confounders and the gradient holds — more sun, fewer deaths, step by step. A confounder would have to track sun dose almost perfectly to fake that.

Confounding is the right worry, and the researchers took it seriously — adjusting for income, education, exercise, smoking, comorbidity. The association didn't vanish; it stayed, and it stayed graded: moderate sun beat avoidance, high sun beat moderate. Invoking an unmeasured confounder is easy to say and hard to specify — you'd need a hidden factor that rises smoothly with sun exposure across the whole range and isn't captured by any of the socioeconomic variables already in the model. Meanwhile there's a mechanism sitting right there: sunlight lowers blood pressure through nitric oxide, and blood pressure is the leading driver of the cardiovascular deaths that move all-cause mortality. When a graded association lines up with a plausible mechanism, "it's probably just rich people on vacation" stops being the parsimonious explanation and starts being the hopeful one.

▸ it's the person, not the photon
Healthy people seek the sun; the sun didn't make them healthy
Sun-seeking is a marker of a whole healthy, affluent, outdoor life. Observational adjustment never fully removes that, and the one thing that could — a randomized trial — hasn't been done.

Ask who lies at the sunny end of that gradient. The person who holidays in the sun, gardens, walks at lunch, and isn't housebound by illness or poverty. Sun exposure is one of the purest markers of a healthy, active, well-off life there is — and reverse causation bites hard, because sick people stay indoors. You can adjust for measured income and exercise, but you're adjusting with blunt survey categories that never capture the full bundle; residual confounding is exactly what you'd expect to leak through and it explains a graded association just fine. The gold standard here would be to randomize people to more or less sun and follow the deaths — and that trial doesn't exist, partly because you can't ethically assign a carcinogen. Until it does, a mortality claim resting on observational data plus a blood-pressure mechanism is a hypothesis, not a license to tell fair-skinned people to bake.

the crux Is the mortality gap causal sunlight or residual confounding by the healthy, affluent lives that seek the sun? ↳ this is Crux 01's ledger fought over one number — whether the benefit column is real or an artifact of who's in it.
what would move them

The seek-it side concedes if the dose-response flattens once fuller measures of wealth, fitness, and baseline health are added, or reverse causation (sick people staying in) explains the gap.

The shade-it side concedes if the graded mortality benefit persists under strong confounder control, quasi-experimental designs, or a nitric-oxide outcomes trial.

Crux 04 — the advicepublic health · trust · crossways

Was "avoid the sun, wear sunscreen daily" sound public health, or dermatology overfitting to its own specialty?

Before scoring the biology, ask what to make of the advice itself — a hard-won safety message, or a one-size rule written by the one specialty that only sees the skin?

▸ a simple rule that saved skin
The message worked — don't unravel it for nuance
Blanket sun-protection advice is legible, safe, and demonstrably cut skin-cancer rates where it was pushed hardest. Public health needs rules people can follow, not personalized dosing nobody will.

Judge the advice by what it was for and what it did. Australia ran the boldest version — Slip, Slop, Slap — and melanoma trends in younger cohorts bent the right way. A public-health message has to survive contact with millions of ordinary people, which means it has to be simple: "don't burn, cover up, wear sunscreen" is a rule a parent can actually apply at a beach. The downside is genuinely small — worst case, take a cheap vitamin D pill. Now weigh the alternative the critics offer: "calculate your personal non-burning dose by skin type, latitude, and season." That's not advice, it's a homework assignment, and in practice it becomes a permission slip to tan that the tanning industry will happily amplify. The nuance is real in a seminar and dangerous on a billboard. Keep the rule; treat the exceptions in the clinic.

▸ the specialist optimized its own organ
One organ's experts wrote the whole body's rule
Dermatology was handed a whole-body question and answered it for the skin, because that's the endpoint it sees, funds, and is measured on. A rule that ignores latitude, skin color, and the rest of the body was overfit from the start.

Notice who wrote the advice and what they're accountable for. Dermatologists are judged on skin-cancer rates, not on blood pressure or all-cause mortality — so naturally they minimized the variable they own and discounted the columns they don't. That's not corruption; it's specialization doing what specialization does, and it's exactly why a single organ's guild shouldn't set a whole-body policy alone. The one-rule-for-everyone framing then papers over enormous variation: it tells a dark-skinned person at high latitude — at real risk of deficiency and at low melanoma risk — the same thing it tells a redhead in Queensland. Holick was pushed out of a dermatology chair for saying so. When the field's response to internal dissent is to remove the dissenter, that's a signal the rule is being defended as turf, not just as science. A whole-body question deserves a whole-body panel.

the crux Is blanket avoidance advice good public health under uncertainty, or a specialty overfitting a whole-body question to the skin? ↳ this one runs crossways — it's about how much to trust the advice-givers before any single study is weighed.
what would move them

The shade-it side concedes if the net systemic harm of blanket avoidance is shown to exceed the skin-cancer it prevents, so the simple rule does more harm than good.

The seek-it side concedes if personalized dosing proves unworkable or dangerous at population scale, making a simple protective default the better public-health bet even if it's imperfect.

IV  the payoff

Four cruxes, two questions underneath

Most of the disagreement sorts onto two axes. The horizontal asks the verdict: shade the sun, or seek it. The vertical asks something prior — which endpoint counts, the skin alone or the whole body. Plot the camps and the field stops looking like one argument. It looks like people standing on different squares, often answering a question their opponent never asked: a dermatologist counting skin and a cardiologist counting deaths can both be right and still disagree forever.

Vitamin, or the light

Crux 03 · the confounder
Photon or person

Does the mortality gap survive who seeks the sun. The horizontal verdict, judged by causal inference.

Find your own hinge. Most of the disagreement is downstream of two things — whether you count the skin or the whole body, and whether the good the sun does is a molecule you can bottle or a dose you have to stand in. Locate which one is doing the work for you, and you'll usually find the other person standing on the other axis.

On these summaries. This is an educational map of a live scientific debate, not medical advice — for what's right for your skin, your latitude, and your health, talk to a clinician who knows them. Two things in this map are not really in dispute and shouldn't be read as "up for grabs": UV causes skin cancer, and tanning beds and sunburns are best avoided. The open questions are about the systemic ledger, the mechanism, and the one-size advice. Each steelman is compressed; the real arguments run to trials and books, and a card or a panel is a doorway, not the room. Dates mark a representative moment, not the first time anyone raised the point. Because the science is still moving, specifics here will age. The two-pole axis is a lens for seeing structure, useful precisely because it simplifies, and untrue to any view that won't sit on a line — which is what the lilac hinge is there to flag.
