FIELD · ethics & moral psychology

Are we made good by habit — or by what we intend?

The oldest question in ethics of character, mapped. A dozen positions on what makes a person and an action good fold into four disagreements, and those into two questions: does moral weight fall on settled character or the present will — and do stable character traits even exist? Below — the lineage, the positions and who held them, and four cruxes, each with the strongest case both sides actually make.

One axis runs under all of it. Watch the two colors.
Habit & characterthe trained disposition · what you reliably do Will & intentionthe reason you act · what you mean to do
I  the lineage

One quarrel, from ritual to the good will

Read top to bottom. A dot's halo marks where the thinker stands — green for character formed by habit, gold for goodness located in the will, lilac for the view that the two can't be pulled apart.

c. 500 BCE
Confuciusethics · ritual
In the Analects, the person of virtue (ren) is formed through li — ritual and practice repeated until it reshapes what one wants. Character is not declared but drilled; you become humane by rehearsing humane conduct until it is yours.
c. 350 BCE
Aristotlephilosophy · the founder
The Nicomachean Ethics: moral virtue "comes about as a result of habit." We become just by doing just acts, temperate by doing temperate acts — yet he adds a catch that seeds the whole hinge: the act must be chosen, for its own sake, from a firm character. A settled state concerned with choice.
c. 100 CE
Epictetus & the Stoicsphilosophy · the will
Training (askēsis) matters, but the good sits in the soul's rational assent — the judgments that are wholly "up to us." Externals, and even trained feeling, are secondary to what the mind chooses to affirm. The locus of virtue slides toward the will.
c. 400
Augustinetheology · love & will
Goodness is a matter of rightly-ordered love and a reordered will — "love, and do what you will." Habit (consuetudo) appears mostly as a chain, the weight of old sin, broken not by more drilling but by grace turning the will around.
c. 1270
Thomas Aquinastheology · habitus
Virtue is a habitus — but a habit perfected by, and expressing, reason and will. The acquired disposition and the intending intellect are not rivals; they are one operation. The clearest medieval statement that the split is a mistake.
1785
Immanuel Kantphilosophy · the good will
The Groundwork: nothing is good without qualification but a good will. An act has moral worth only when done from duty, for the sake of the law — and a good deed done from habit or inclination earns none of it. Kant even warns that habit drains actions of their worth by dulling the mind's freedom.
1958
G. E. M. Anscombephilosophy · revival
"Modern Moral Philosophy" detonates the law-without-a-lawgiver "moral ought" of Kant and the utilitarians, and sends philosophy back to the virtues and to what a good human life is. The starting gun for the neo-Aristotelian revival.
1978
Philippa Footphilosophy · neo-Aristotelian
Virtues and Vices rebuilds virtue as a genuine feature of a good human life, not a mask over self-interest. Character traits — not rules and not consequences — become the primary bearers of moral value again.
1999
Rosalind Hursthousephilosophy · virtue ethics
On Virtue Ethics gives the view a full action-guiding form: the right act is what a person of virtuous character would characteristically do. Habituated character does the normative heavy lifting.
1999
Harman & Dorispsychology · situationism
The empirical broadside: Milgram, the Good Samaritan seminarians, the dime in the phone booth. Behavior tracks trivial situational features far more than any cross-situational "trait" — so the robust character the whole tradition leans on may barely exist.
2011
Julia Annasphilosophy · the skill model
Intelligent Virtue presses the fusion: virtue is like a practical skill — a pianist's, a doctor's — habituated but intelligent, reason-responsive, articulate. Not a reflex and not a bare resolve, but a trained way of choosing well. The hinge, argued in full.
Into the present

Few now defend the crude split: the mainstream reading holds that mature virtue is a trained disposition to choose well, so habit and intention are two faces of one thing. The live fight has moved underneath — to the empirical question of whether robust character traits exist at all.

II  the positions

Who holds what — and where it sits on the axis

Two families: those who locate goodness in settled character and those who locate it in the will. A card's left edge is its lean; the tag is rough sociology, not a scorecard.

The primacy of character habit & disposition

◀ habit Aristotelian virtue ethics the founding view, now dominant in the field
Aristotle · Foot · Hursthouse · MacIntyre
Goodness is a hexis — a settled state built by habituation. The virtuous person reliably does the right thing, does it gladly, and does it because that is the kind of person practice has made them.
Note the built-in caveat: for Aristotle the act must also be chosen for its own sake — which is why this view keeps sliding toward the hinge.
SEP: Aristotle's Ethics
◀ habit Confucian ritual & role ethics the East Asian mainstream
Confucius · Xunzi · Mencius
Character (ren) is cultivated through li — repeated ritual and role-practice that gradually retunes desire itself, so that in the end one wants what is right.
Xunzi presses hardest on drill; Mencius roots it in innate "sprouts" that practice extends — a family argument inside the habit camp.
SEP: Confucius
◀ habit Neo-Aristotelian revival the mainstream of academic virtue ethics
Anscombe · Foot · Hursthouse · Slote
After Anscombe's 1958 attack on rule-based ethics, right action is defined by reference to character: what the person of practical wisdom would characteristically do.
Restores the agent, not the act or its results, to the center of moral theory.
◆ hinge Thomist habitus the classical synthesis
Aquinas · Anscombe · MacIntyre
Virtue is a habit — but one informed and directed by reason and will. The trained disposition and the intending mind are a single perfected power, not two competitors.
Straddles both poles on purpose: it exists to deny that habit and intention come apart.

The primacy of the will intention & assent

▶ intent Kantian deontology the dominant modern rival
Kant · Korsgaard · O'Neill
The only unconditional good is a good will. Moral worth lies in the maxim — in acting from duty for the law's sake — and a good act done from habit or inclination has none of it.
Kant treats settled habit with suspicion: automatic goodness, on his view, is exactly what freedom and worth are not.
SEP: Kant's Moral Philosophy
▶ intent Stoic rationalism the ancient counter-current
Epictetus · Seneca · Marcus Aurelius
Virtue is the soul's right assent — the rational judgment that alone is "up to us." Trained feeling helps, but the good is the will affirming what reason sees, not the groove of behavior.
Askēsis is real training, but it serves the judgment; the verdict, not the reflex, is where virtue lives.
▶ intent Augustinian voluntarism the Christian inward turn
Augustine · Bernard · Luther
Goodness is a rightly-ordered will and rightly-directed love; what you mean and love is the heart of the act. Habit figures mainly as the chain of sin, undone by grace, not by more repetition.
"Love, and do what you will" makes the inner orientation, not the outward drill, decisive.
▶ skeptic Situationism & character skepticism the live empirical challenge
Harman · Doris · Merritt
Robust, cross-situational character traits are largely a fiction; conduct is driven by circumstance. If so, the "habit" the first family leans on may not exist — leaving momentary, situation-aware intention as the only real lever.
Not a fourth virtue theory but a solvent poured on the whole debate — which is why it runs crossways below.
SEP: Moral Character — Empirical Approaches
the hinge ↕
The view that the dichotomy is false

Aristotle's own definition already fuses the poles: virtue is a hexis concerned with prohairesis — a settled disposition to choose well, knowingly and for its own sake. A drilled reflex that can't say why it acts is, for him, mere "continence," not virtue; a warm intention that never sets into reliable conduct is only a wish. Aquinas's habitus and Julia Annas's skill model make the same move from opposite eras: mature virtue is intelligent habit, a trained way of intending that stays responsive to reasons, the way a musician's or a doctor's expertise is both practiced and alert. On this reading "habit or intention?" mistakes two aspects of one thing for two rival candidates — and the interesting question is not which wins but how they lock together.

Tags are rough sociology — how the literature shakes out — not a verdict. Positions on both sides have held the field at different times; popularity and truth keep separate books.
III  the cruxes

Four fair fights, each carrying part of the load

The positions are bundles of answers; these are the questions that, answered one way or the other, split them. Both panels give the side's best case, in its own voice.

Crux 01 — the worthvalue

Where does an action's moral worth come from — the character it flows from, or the reason behind it?

Two people give the same coin to the same beggar. One is generous; the other is calculating. Are they morally equal?

▸ worth is in the disposition
Virtue as reliable character
An act is good because it flows from a settled good character; the person who reliably does right, without fuss, is the one we rightly call good.

When a generous person gives, they don't first consult a principle — giving is simply what a generous person does, and that reliability is the virtue. It's also the only thing a friend or a community can actually lean on. Intentions, by contrast, are cheap, private, and endlessly self-flattering: everyone means well at 6 a.m. What reveals a person is the grain of conduct worn in over years, not the resolution formed this morning. "The road to hell is paved with good intentions" is not cynicism — it is the plain observation that meaning well and doing well come apart, and only the second is character you can trust.

▸ worth is in the will
The good will (Kant)
An act has moral worth only when done for the right reason; a good deed done from mere habit or inclination earns no more credit than a well-made machine.

Kant's shopkeeper gives honest change — but only because cheating would cost him custom. He does the right thing and deserves nothing for it; his honesty is prudence wearing virtue's coat. Set beside him the man who is honest when it hurts, from duty alone: there the moral worth shines, visible precisely because inclination isn't doing the work. A habit is a kind of mechanism, and a mechanism can't be praised — only its maker can. If your goodness is just conditioning that happened to take, the credit belongs to your upbringing, not to you. Worth requires that the good be willed, not merely emitted.

the crux Does moral worth reside in the reliable disposition or the reason one acts? Who deserves praise, and what we try to instill in children, both turn on this.
what would move them

The character side concedes if a person who acts rightly with no good reason at all — mechanically, unable to say why — is shown to be as admirable as one who acts from understanding.

The will side concedes if reliable good conduct is shown to be genuinely praiseworthy even with no conscious moral intention behind it — if "doing good without thinking" can be a virtue and not just a lucky reflex.

Crux 02 — the makingmoral education

How do people actually become good — by doing before understanding, or by understanding before doing?

Hand the same child two upbringings: one drills the conduct, the other reasons it out. Which one raises virtue?

▸ practice comes first
Habituation (Aristotle)
You acquire virtue the way you learn an instrument — by doing the acts first; the right feelings and the understanding follow the drill, not the reverse.

"We become just by doing just acts, temperate by doing temperate acts," Aristotle writes — we don't first grasp justice in the abstract and then deploy it, any more than a builder learns to build by lecture. Children are held to honesty long before they can argue for it, and the practice slowly reshapes what they want, until the good feels natural rather than imposed. Insight without the groove is inert: everyone already knows they ought to be patient. The person who has drilled patience for twenty years is the one who actually has it. Character is carved by repetition — you act your way into right feeling, not the other way round.

▸ understanding comes first
Conditioning isn't virtue
A disposition installed without understanding is mere training — you'd have shaped a person the way you shape a dog. Real virtue must be grasped and endorsed, or it isn't the agent's own.

Drill a child into compliant habits and you may get reliable behavior, but you have not yet produced a moral agent — you've produced a well-trained animal whose "virtue" evaporates the instant the training's assumptions fail. What makes conduct yours is that you understand and endorse it; the will has to be engaged, or the goodness belongs to whoever ran the conditioning. And habit is a neutral engine: the cruel become cruel by practice just as surely as the kind become kind. Repetition on its own entrenches whatever it's fed. Without a reason the agent can grasp and own, habituation is as likely to set vice as virtue — understanding has to be there to steer.

the crux Does virtue come from practice preceding insight or insight governing practice? At stake is whether moral education is fundamentally drill or dialogue.
what would move them

The practice side concedes if habituation without any understanding is shown to entrench vice about as readily as virtue — so that the drill, by itself, guarantees nothing.

The understanding side concedes if children are shown to become reliably good through practice before they can grasp or endorse the reasons — so that understanding follows habit rather than founding it.

Crux 03 — the effortagency · freedom

Is the ideal to do good effortlessly and automatically, or to do good as a fresh, free choice each time?

One person does the right thing without a second thought; another does it after wrestling down the urge to bolt. Which is the better human being?

▸ second nature is the summit
Effortless virtue
The fully virtuous person does right without struggle and takes pleasure in it; having to grit your teeth is a lower grade of goodness, not a higher one.

Aristotle draws a sharp line between the virtuous person, who does the generous thing gladly, and the merely continent one, who does it while pinning down a contrary desire. The second is admirable but incomplete — still at war with himself. The summit is the disposition so settled that the right response arrives unbidden, the way a master reads a board at a glance. To demand that every good act be a fresh effort of will is to freeze everyone at the beginner's stage and then call the trembling the achievement. In every other domain we recognize that mastery is the disappearance of struggle; morality is no exception.

▸ freedom is the whole point
The willed act (Kant)
Goodness on autopilot is brittle and, worse, unfree; only an act that could have been chosen otherwise is one the agent can answer for.

The person who does good against inclination shows the will at its purest — which is exactly why Kant finds moral worth blazing where habit and desire pull the other way. Automatic virtue is also fragile: it doesn't adapt when the situation shifts under it, and someone running on grooves can't truly answer for what the grooves do. Kant warned outright that habit "deprives even good actions of their moral worth" by impairing the mind's freedom. Responsibility requires that each act stay, in principle, a live choice — not a reflex worn smooth. A saint who couldn't do otherwise is not a saint but a mechanism that happens to be aimed well.

the crux Is the good life effortless mastery or perpetual free choice? The dispute is whether struggle marks virtue's shortfall or its dignity.
what would move them

The mastery side concedes if effortless, unstruggling good conduct is shown to be less admirable — or less free — than the same conduct done against inclination.

The freedom side concedes if the person for whom goodness has become second nature is shown to be fully free and responsible in it — so that the absence of struggle costs nothing in agency.

Crux 04 — the realitypsychology · crossways

Before asking whether habit or intention weighs more — is "character" even real?

The first three cruxes assume there are honest people and cowardly ones. The psychology lab asks whether that assumption survives contact with the evidence.

▸ character is real
Trait realism
People do have robust dispositions — the honest, the coward, the kind — and we predict, trust, and build our lives around them every day, mostly rightly.

We are not fooled at random. We lend to the reliable, confide in the discreet, keep our distance from the cruel — and across years and settings we are mostly right. The famous experiments show that decent people can be pushed into bad acts under engineered pressure; they do not show that there is no difference between people. That some situations overwhelm most characters was never news to Aristotle, who thought full virtue rare and hard-won. Fragility at the extremes is entirely compatible with real, stable traits in the ordinary run of life — which is the only place character was ever claimed to operate.

▸ character is largely a fiction
Situationism (Harman, Doris)
The evidence says behavior tracks trivial situational features far more than any cross-situational trait — so the "character" the whole debate leans on may barely exist.

Whether you stop to help a stranger turns out to hinge on whether you just found a dime, or passed a bakery, or were told you're running late — not on how "kind" you are. Milgram's subjects, ordinary people, delivered what they believed were lethal shocks because a man in a coat told them to continue. If conduct is this hostage to circumstance, then "the honest person" is a summary of past luck, not a reliable engine of future acts. And if robust traits are largely a fiction, the habit-versus-intention contest is built on sand: what's left to work with is deliberate, situation-aware intention — noticing the pressure you're under and choosing against it.

the crux Are cross-situational character traits real, or an attribution error? ↳ this runs crossways to Cruxes 01–03 — it asks whether the "habit" they all presuppose even exists.
what would move them

The trait-realist side concedes if rigorous studies find no stable, cross-situational differences in conduct once the situation is controlled — no honest-vs-dishonest signal that survives the setting.

The situationist side concedes if character traits are shown to predict behavior across varied, uncontrived situations better than the situation does — so that "character" earns its explanatory keep.

IV  the payoff

Four cruxes, two questions underneath

Cruxes 01–03 are three angles on a single question: when we judge a person or an act, does the weight fall on the trained disposition or on the present will — on what you reliably do, or on why you do it? Crux 04 runs crossways: it asks whether the disposition the first three lean on is even real. Plot both and the field stops looking like one long duel between Aristotle and Kant. It looks like people standing on different squares — and, as often as not, refusing a choice that isn't forced, since mature virtue is a habit of intending well.

Crux 01 · worth
Where worth lives

The horizontal axis, asked of a single act. In the reliable disposition, or in the reason one wills it.

Crux 04 · reality
Is character real

The vertical axis. Whether the stable trait the other three lean on exists at all — the question that can pull the floor out.

Crux 02 · formation
How virtue is made

The horizontal axis again, aimed at upbringing. Practice before insight, or insight governing practice.

Crux 03 · effort
Automatic or free

The horizontal axis stress-tested. Effortless second nature, or the freely willed act.

Find your own hinge. Few people are torn on all four — one carries the weight. Locate it, work out whether you're really arguing about what a person reliably is or about what they mean in the moment — and you may find, with Aristotle and Kant both, that the deepest answer refuses the choice.
