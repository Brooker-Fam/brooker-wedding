FIELD · energy, the grid & the economics of compute

The AI build-out: essential infrastructure — or a costly overreach?

A trillion dollars of compute is going up faster than the wires, the water, and the revenue can keep pace. A dozen positions on the data-center boom fold into four disagreements, and those into two questions: how much will the AI itself actually deliver, and who ends up paying for the power and water? Below — the lineage, the positions and who holds them, and four cruxes, each with the strongest case both sides make. At stake: your electricity bill, river levels in drought country, climate targets, and the US–China race.

I  the lineage

How compute-on-tap became a power-grid problem

Read top to bottom. A dot's halo marks where the moment lands — green for the case that the build-out is under control or worth it, gold for the case that it's outrunning what the grid and the public can bear, lilac for the in-between.

1961
John McCarthycomputing · utility
At MIT's centennial the AI pioneer floats an idea ahead of its hardware: computation sold like electricity or water, metered and on tap. The seed of everything that followed — compute as a utility, with a utility's appetite.
2006
Amazon launches EC2industry · the cloud
Rentable servers by the hour turn McCarthy's metaphor into a market. The hyperscale era starts here; compute stops being something you own and becomes something you draw from a few enormous buildings.
2011
Open Compute Projectengineering · efficiency
Facebook open-sources its data-center designs and the industry chases efficiency hard. Power Usage Effectiveness — overhead energy per unit of compute — falls toward ~1.1 at the best sites. For a decade, efficiency is the answer to every worry about footprint.
c. 2018
The flat-load decadegrid · baseline
US data-center electricity use stays roughly flat through the 2010s even as traffic explodes — efficiency gains eat the growth. This is the "it's handled" baseline every later alarm has to argue against.
Nov 2022
ChatGPT shipsAI · the scramble
A research preview hits a hundred million users in two months and rewrites every hyperscaler's capital plan. Training a frontier model, then serving it to everyone, turns compute demand from steady into vertical.
2023
The H100 boomhardware · demand
Nvidia's data-center GPUs become the scarce input of the decade; allocation, not money, is the bottleneck. A rack of these draws what a small neighborhood does, and everyone wants thousands.
2024
The Berkeley Lab reportpolicy · the grid
Lawrence Berkeley National Lab puts US data centers at ~4.4% of national electricity in 2023, with a path to 6.7–12% by 2028. Grid operators start filing warnings; the flat-load era is officially over.
Sept 2024
Three Mile Island restartenergy · nuclear
Microsoft and Constellation sign a 20-year deal to reopen the undamaged Unit 1 at Three Mile Island — shut in 2019 on economics — to feed AI. The clearest sign yet that demand might pull firm clean power back from the dead.
Jan 2025
"Stargate" announcedcapital · the buildout
OpenAI, Oracle, and SoftBank unveil a $500B US data-center program. Transformer and gas-turbine order books stretch years out; some utilities file rate cases citing data-center load. The scale stops being abstract.
2025–26
The reckoning beginsgrid · water · rules
The IEA projects global data-center generation past 1,000 TWh by 2030. Water use draws fire — researchers tally roughly 264 billion gallons for AI in 2025. In March 2026 several developers sign a "Ratepayer Protection Pledge" to cover the full cost of generation they require; states trade moratoria and incentives.
Into the present

The build-out is racing ahead of the grid, the water, and the revenue all at once. Whether this is the new railroads — durable infrastructure for a technology that pays off — or the new telecom glut of 2001, fiber laid into a crash, is the question nobody can answer yet.

II  the positions

Who holds what — and where it sits on the axis

Two families of position: one starts from the costs the build-out imposes, the other from the upside it promises. A card's left edge is its lean; the tag is rough sociology, not a scorecard.

On the costs energy · water · money

▶ rein in Grid-strain & ratepayer critics rising fast in policy
consumer advocates · state utility commissions · some grid operators
Demand is outpacing the supply of plants and lines, and the cost of building both gets folded into everyone's rates — so an ordinary household pays part of the bill for a data center it will never use.
The fight is largely over cost allocation: who in the rate base is on the hook for new generation.
▶ rein in Climate critics prominent among energy researchers
climate scientists · environmental advocates
To meet the load fast, utilities are building new gas, keeping coal online past its retirement date, and burning through carbon budgets that were already tight.
Concedes some of the new power is clean; the worry is the marginal megawatt, which is often fossil.
▶ rein in Water & community critics growing at the local level
host-town residents · water-district officials · journalists
A hyperscale site can draw millions of gallons a day for cooling — sometimes in places already short of water — and leaves behind a few dozen permanent jobs after the construction crews go home.
Often fought parcel by parcel, in zoning hearings rather than national debate.
Li et al., "Making AI Less Thirsty"
◀ build Clean-abundance optimists growing in energy circles
nuclear advocates · some utilities · clean-firm developers
Large, creditworthy, around-the-clock demand is the best thing to happen to clean firm power in a generation — it funds nuclear restarts, SMRs, and geothermal that wouldn't pencil out otherwise.
Points to the Three Mile Island restart as proof the demand pulls real projects forward.

On the upside growth · security · the AI itself

◀ build Build-the-future techno-optimists dominant in the AI industry
frontier labs · most hyperscaler leadership
AI is a general-purpose technology and compute is the binding constraint on it. The expensive mistake isn't overbuilding — it's underbuilding and finding you can't catch up.
Reads today's capex the way an earlier era read electrification: a one-time cost for a permanent capability.
◀ build National-competitiveness hawks strong across both US parties
defense thinkers · policymakers · Leopold Aschenbrenner
Compute is a strategic resource. Losing the AI frontier to China is the real risk, and keeping the frontier at home keeps its rules and its safeguards at home too.
Treats power and chips as national assets, not just private capex.
Aschenbrenner, "Situational Awareness"
▶ rein in Bubble skeptics loud among investors since 2024
Wall Street analysts · Sequoia's David Cahn
Roughly $700B a year of capex is running far ahead of what AI actually earns. If the returns disappoint, you get stranded GPUs, idle buildings, and a cascade through everyone who lent against them.
Not anti-AI as such — the claim is about the gap between spend and revenue.
Sequoia, "AI's $600B Question"
▶ rein in "Do we need this?" skeptics a minority, but vocal
efficiency researchers · degrowth-leaning critics
Question the demand itself. Smaller models, better algorithms, and inference on cheaper hardware could meet most real needs without the megaprojects and the power plants to feed them.
Where the others argue about how to build, this side asks whether to.
the hinge ↕
Build smarter — make them pay their way

One stance takes the build-out as basically necessary but puts conditions on it. Data centers bring their own clean generation, or pay the full grid cost they create. They cool with closed-loop or air systems instead of drinking the local river. They run flexibly and curtail when the grid is tight, since a load that can pause is worth far more than one that can't. They site away from scarce water, and they open their numbers so regulators aren't guessing. The March 2026 Ratepayer Protection Pledge and the "bring-your-own-power" deals — where a developer signs for a reactor or a gas plant before it draws a watt from the public grid — are the first real moves in this direction. Whether they hold, or stay press releases, is the open question.

Tags are rough sociology — how loud and how widely held a position is right now — not a verdict on who's right. This is a fast-moving fight; the balance has shifted twice in three years and will shift again.
III  the cruxes

Four fair fights, each carrying part of the load

The positions are bundles of answers; these are the questions that, answered one way or the other, split them. Both panels give the side's best case, in its own voice.

Crux 01 — the gridenergy · economics

Can the grid take it — and who pays?

Demand is climbing faster than US power has grown in twenty years. Does that pull clean power forward, or socialize dirty, expensive power onto everyone else?

▸ the strongest pull for new power in a generation
Demand builds the supply
A creditworthy buyer who'll take power around the clock for twenty years is exactly what clean firm generation needs to get financed — and the build-out is starting to pay its own way.

Clean firm power — nuclear, geothermal, the long-duration stuff — has always died on financing, not physics. Nobody builds a reactor on spec. AI changed that: a hyperscaler will sign a two-decade offtake before the first concrete is poured, which is why a shuttered reactor at Three Mile Island is coming back and why developers have lined up something like 45 GW of nuclear offtake interest. Data-center load is also unusually flexible — training can pause, and operators can curtail at peak if the price signal is right. And the industry is moving toward covering its own costs: the March 2026 Ratepayer Protection Pledge has developers promising to pay for the generation they require rather than spreading it across the rate base.

▸ the bill lands on people who never asked for it
The costs get socialized
Demand is outrunning supply, the fastest fix is gas, and the cost of new plants and wires gets folded into rates that have already climbed faster than inflation.

You can't conjure a reactor in the eighteen months a data center wants power, so the load gets met with what's available — new gas, and coal plants kept past their retirement dates. Meanwhile the cost of new generation and transmission flows into the rate base, and US electric bills have been rising faster than inflation with data centers named as a driver. "Flexible" is mostly a promise; few operators have actually curtailed at scale, because every idle hour is wasted capital. A pledge signed in 2026 is not a tariff, and not a law. Until the meter proves otherwise, the public is the backstop for a private build-out.

the crux Does AI demand pull clean abundance forward, or socialize dirty, expensive power onto the public and the climate? Most of the energy fight is downstream of this.
what would move them

The build side concedes if the demand is shown to lock in fossil generation and raise ordinary ratepayers' bills, with no offsetting clean build-out.

The restraint side concedes if data-center demand is shown to pull forward clean firm power that wouldn't otherwise get built, and operators pay their own grid costs.

Crux 02 — the groundwater · community

What does a host town actually get?

A site lands on a county's tax rolls and on its water table at the same time. Is it a windfall, or an extraction?

▸ investment, tax base, and a lighter footprint than you think
A neighbor worth having
A data center drops billions of capital and a long-term tax base into places that rarely see either, and newer sites barely touch local water.

A single campus is a multi-billion-dollar investment, years of construction work, and a property-tax stream a rural county can use to fund its schools. The water complaint is aimed at an older design: the newest builds run closed-loop cooling that recirculates the same water, or air cooling that uses almost none, or recycled greywater that never competed with drinking supply. Communities aren't powerless here — they negotiate the terms, the tax abatements, the water agreements. A town that drives a hard bargain can come out well ahead, and many have chosen to.

▸ it drinks the water and leaves
An extractive draw
A hyperscale site can use up to five million gallons of water a day, often where water is already scarce, in exchange for a handful of permanent jobs and a deal the public never gets to read.

Evaporative cooling at a big site can consume up to five million gallons a day, and the new builds are landing in exactly the dry, cheap-land regions where that's hardest to spare. The tax windfall gets eaten by abatements handed over to win the project, and the jobs number collapses once construction ends — a few dozen technicians run a building the size of several stadiums. The deals are signed under NDAs, so residents learn the water terms after the fact. Runs hot, runs loud, draws the aquifer down, and the company can pack up the racks and move when the incentives dry up.

the crux Is a data center a tax windfall and a good neighbor, or an extractive draw on scarce local water and land? The answer changes site by site — which is why this fights parcel by parcel.
what would move them

The build side concedes if host communities are shown to get little lasting benefit while bearing real water and infrastructure costs.

The restraint side concedes if operators are shown to use closed-loop or air cooling and deliver tax base and grid investment that towns actually value.

Crux 03 — the racegeopolitics · crossways

Is a maximal domestic build-out a national imperative?

"National security" is either the reason to build everything, or the phrase that ends the argument before it starts.

▸ compute is the strategic resource of the era
Win the race or lose the rules
Falling behind China on AI is a security and economic threat, and keeping the frontier on US soil keeps its safeguards on US soil too.

The lead in AI runs through compute the way an earlier century's ran through steel and oil. A rival that reaches decisive capability first sets the terms — in weapons, in cyber, in the economy that funds both. Ceding that to a strategic competitor isn't a market outcome you can shrug at; it's a posture you can't easily reverse. Keeping the frontier domestic also keeps the rules domestic: the safety regimes, the export controls, the audits all live wherever the biggest clusters do. Build at home, fast, or watch the capability — and the say over how it's used — move somewhere with fewer scruples.

▸ the phrase that launders the externalities
A blank check for the build-out
"National security" turns every cost — the water, the bills, the emissions — into an acceptable price, and skips the prior question of whether we need this much compute at all.

Wrap a project in the flag and the externalities stop counting. The race framing converts a question that deserves scrutiny — should we socialize this power, drain this aquifer, build this much — into a settled yes, because the alternative is "losing to China." But the strategic case rarely specifies how much domestic compute the security actually requires; it just says more. Efficiency gains, smaller specialized models, or selective offshoring of non-sensitive workloads might secure the genuine national interest at a fraction of the footprint. The arms-race frame forecloses that conversation. The hard part isn't building; it's admitting how little of the build the security case truly needs.

the crux Is maximal domestic compute a genuine strategic necessity, or a rationale that ends the debate before it starts? ↳ this runs crossways — it ties to how much you think the AI itself will deliver (Crux 04).
what would move them

The build side concedes if "national security" is shown to be a blank check — the strategic need doesn't actually require this much domestic compute.

The restraint side concedes if ceding compute leadership is shown to carry real security and economic costs that efficiency or offshoring can't avoid.

Crux 04 — the payoffmarkets · crossways

Will the AI pay off enough to justify the build-out?

This is the second axis the other three ride on. Railroads, or fiber laid into the 2001 crash?

▸ infrastructure for a general-purpose technology
Build too little, too late, is the real risk
Models keep improving and the demand for running them is compounding; this is the railroads, where the danger was always underinvestment.

Every capability jump opens uses that didn't exist the quarter before, and each one adds inference load that never goes away. The history of general-purpose technologies — rail, the grid, broadband — is that the infrastructure looks overbuilt right up until it looks obviously too small, and the firms that hesitated never caught the leaders who didn't. A GPU bought today earns while it runs; the cluster is a productive asset, not a bet on a single product. If the AI is anything like what the last three years suggest, the people warning about overcapacity will look the way people who warned about "too much internet" look now.

▸ the spend is running ahead of the revenue
A capex bubble we're pre-paying
Hundreds of billions a year are going into hardware that earns a fraction of it back, and depreciates fast enough that it may be obsolete before it pays for itself.

Roughly $700B of hyperscaler capex in 2026 needs an enormous, recurring revenue stream to justify it — and the AI revenue, real as it is, lags far behind. The hardware doesn't wait for the math to work: a GPU is largely written off in a few years, and the next generation makes the last one a discount item. So the clock is brutal. If demand or pricing disappoints even modestly, you're left with stranded silicon, half-empty buildings, power contracts signed for load that never shows, and the lenders who financed it all looking for the exit at once. The dot-com fiber glut is the cautionary tale: useful in the end, ruinous on the way there.

the crux Durable infrastructure for a transformative technology, or a capex bubble whose costs we are pre-paying? This is the second axis the others ride on.
what would move them

The build side concedes if AI revenue keeps badly lagging capex and the hardware is obsoleted fast, leaving stranded assets.

The restraint side concedes if inference demand and model capability keep compounding, making today's build-out look like underinvestment in hindsight.

IV  the payoff

Four cruxes, two questions underneath

Most of the fight runs on two axes. One is left–right: build it out, or rein it in — the question of who pays for the power and water. The other is up–down: is the AI payoff real and worth it, or overhyped? Cruxes 03 and 04 are mostly the vertical question; Cruxes 01 and 02 sit on the horizontal one. Plot the camps and the field stops looking like one argument. People are standing on different squares, often answering a question the other side never asked.

Most of the fight is downstream of two things — how much you believe the AI itself will deliver, and who you think ends up paying for the power and water. Find which one moves you, and you'll usually find the other person standing on the other axis.
