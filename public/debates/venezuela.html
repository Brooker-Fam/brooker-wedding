FIELD · political economy, the resource curse & the Bolivarian project

Did Venezuela ruin itself — or was it strangled?

A century of oil built the richest country in Latin America; a quarter-century of Chavismo ended with the largest peacetime economic collapse the region has ever recorded, eight million people fled, and — on 3 January 2026 — a U.S. raid that flew the president to a courtroom in New York. The fight over why sorts into two answers: it was wrecked from within — socialism, misrule, a gutted oil company — or from without — U.S. sanctions, the oil crash, an empire that wanted it to fail. Below: the lineage of the rise and the fall, the positions and who holds them, and four cruxes, each with the strongest case both sides actually make. The hinge under all of it is the barrel of oil.

I  the lineage

From the richest country in the region to the largest collapse

Read top to bottom — the boom and the bust, braided with the ideas that frame them. A dot's halo marks where it leans: green for the wounds Venezuela dealt itself, gold for the blows that came from outside, lilac for the thing under both — an economy that was only ever a barrel of oil.

1922
The well at La Rosa blowsoil · the trap is set
A gusher near Maracaibo turns a poor agricultural country into a petrostate almost overnight. Within a decade Venezuela is the world's second-largest oil producer. Everything that follows — the wealth, the dependence, the fragility — grows from the decision the country never really made: to live off one commodity and import the rest.
1958 → 1960
Punto Fijo democracy · OPECinstitutions · the rentier state
A pact among elites ends dictatorship and makes Venezuela a stable democracy — funded by oil rents flowing through the state. Caracas co-founds OPEC in 1960. The model hardens: whoever holds the presidency holds the oil money, and politics becomes the contest to distribute it.
1976
Oil nationalized · PDVSA is bornthe crown jewel
President Carlos Andrés Pérez nationalizes the industry and creates Petróleos de Venezuela (PDVSA), but lets it run as an arm's-length technocracy — meritocratic, professional, world-class. For two decades it is the competent core of the state and the envy of the developing world. Remember this; it is the thing later broken.
1989
The Caracazogrievance · the wound
Pérez, returned to power, imposes an IMF austerity package. Caracas erupts; the army fires on the slums and hundreds — by some counts thousands — are killed. The neoliberal shock and the massacre that answered the protest become the founding grievance of Chavismo: proof, to a generation, that the old order served everyone but the poor.
1992
Chávez's failed coup · "por ahora"the rise begins
A paratrooper colonel, Hugo Chávez, leads a coup against Pérez. It fails, but on live television he takes responsibility and says the cause has lost "por ahora" — for now. The phrase makes him a folk hero. He goes to prison, is pardoned, and turns to the ballot box.
1998 → 1999
Chávez elected · the Bolivarian Republicthe project
Chávez wins the presidency on a promise to end the Punto Fijo order and turn the oil toward the poor. A new 1999 constitution renames the country, refounds its institutions, and concentrates power in the presidency. The Bolivarian Revolution is underway.
Apr 2002
The coup that briefly toppled himempire · the grievance hardens
Business and military factions oust Chávez for 47 hours; the interim leader dissolves the constitution. Washington, almost alone, welcomes the change before a popular and military backlash returns Chávez to power. For Chavismo this is the proof that never fades: the United States will back anyone who moves against the revolution.
2002 → 2003
The PDVSA strike · 18,000 firedthe self-inflicted wound
A management-led oil strike tries to force Chávez out. He survives it and fires roughly 18,000 PDVSA employees — the geologists, reservoir engineers, and managers who were the company's institutional memory — replacing them with political loyalists. The crown jewel is hollowed out. Output never fully recovers; this is the hinge the whole production collapse turns on.
2004 → 2012
The oil boom · the misionesthe apex of the rise
Oil climbs past $100. Chávez pours the windfall into the misiones: clinics in the barrios, a literacy drive UNESCO credited with ending illiteracy, subsidized food, free education. Poverty falls from roughly 49% to the mid-20s; inequality, the lowest in the region. Real gains, lived by millions — funded entirely by a price that would not last, with nothing saved and firms expropriated by the hundreds.
2013
Chávez dies · Maduro inheritssuccession
Chávez dies of cancer and hands the movement to Nicolás Maduro, a former bus driver and foreign minister, who wins a narrow election. Oil is still near $100. The machine is still running — on a price about to break.
2014 → 2016
The oil price crashesthe external shock
Brent falls from over $100 to under $30. An economy that imported nearly everything and saved nothing loses its only source of dollars. GDP contracts almost 5% in 2014 — before any broad sanctions — and the floor gives way. Shortages, price controls, and the printing press follow.
2016 → 2018
Hyperinflationthe collapse, before the oil sanctions
The bolívar dies. Inflation passes 130,000% in 2018; GDP falls by double digits year after year. People lose weight for lack of food — the "Maduro diet." This catastrophe is already in motion while the U.S. is still buying Venezuelan oil: the timing is the single hardest fact in the whole argument.
Aug 2017 → 2019
Financial then oil sanctions · Guaidóthe squeeze tightens
After targeted sanctions on officials from 2015, Washington bars Venezuela from U.S. credit markets in 2017 — blocking any debt restructuring — and in January 2019 sanctions PDVSA itself, cutting off its largest cash market, while recognizing opposition leader Juan Guaidó as president. A deep collapse meets a deliberate financial blockade.
2015 → 2024
The exodusthe human cost
Some 7.7 million Venezuelans flee — the largest displacement in the Western Hemisphere's history, rivaling Syria. At home the economy partly stabilizes through unofficial dollarization, but the recovery is thin and brutally unequal. A 2024 election the opposition's own tally sheets show it winning is claimed by Maduro anyway.
3 Jan 2026
Operation Absolute Resolve · Maduro capturedthe fall
After months of a U.S. military build-up in the Caribbean and lethal strikes on alleged drug boats, American forces raid Caracas, seize Maduro and his wife, and fly them to New York to face narcotics charges. The Supreme Tribunal declares the presidency "temporarily" vacant and Vice-President Delcy Rodríguez assumes it. Chavismo's chief is gone — removed by foreign force, the constitution's succession clause followed to the letter.
Into the present

As of mid-2026 Venezuela is in a "calculated transition." Rodríguez has freed hundreds of political prisoners and courted Washington, which recognized her in March in exchange for cooperation and access to the oil; elections are promised for August. The opposition that won in 2024 — María Corina Machado, Edmundo González — is still pressing for a real handover, not a managed continuity. Whether this ends in democracy, a friendlier Chavismo, or a U.S.-supervised oil client is open today.

II  the positions

Who blames what — and where it sits on the axis

Two families: what caused the collapse, and what the whole project amounted to. A card's left edge is its lean; the tag is rough weight in the argument today, not a scorecard of who's right.

On the cause of the collapse within, or without

◀ within Socialism, predictably the dominant Western read
Ricardo Hausmann · free-market economists · Mises tradition
Price controls, mass expropriation, a printing press run to cover deficits, and the abolition of the price mechanism produced exactly the shortages, capital flight, and hyperinflation the theory predicts. The currency died before Washington touched the oil.
The textbook case: you cannot suspend the laws of supply and demand by decree and expect goods on the shelves.
Mises Institute
◀ within They broke the one thing that worked the oil-economist consensus
Francisco Monaldi · energy analysts
Firing 18,000 PDVSA professionals in 2003 and packing the company with loyalists destroyed the technical core of the state. Oil output fell from ~3 million barrels a day toward 700,000 — a self-inflicted wound, not a market one.
Even with no sanctions and high prices, a hollowed-out PDVSA could not keep the wells flowing.
CFR backgrounder
▶ without Economic war by sanctions the Chavista & left case
Weisbrot & Sachs · Caracas · Francisco Rodríguez (partial)
The 2017 financial sanctions blocked the debt restructuring that could have stopped the spiral; the 2019 oil sanctions cut off the country's main source of dollars for food and medicine. One study tied them to tens of billions in losses and tens of thousands of excess deaths.
A government in crisis was denied the one tool — access to credit and its own oil revenue — that might have arrested it.
Rodríguez case study
▶ without The oil crash & the resource curse the structuralist read
resource-curse economists · development scholars
A one-commodity economy was always one price collapse away from ruin. Brent fell from $100 to under $30 in 2014; the fuse was lit by the market, not by ideology. Petrostates across the political spectrum boom and bust the same way.
Closest to the hinge: it points past both poles to the barrel of oil under everything.

On the project & its end what Chavismo was

◀ within An authoritarian wrecking project the liberal-democratic verdict
opposition · democracy scholars · most of the press
Chávez and Maduro packed the courts, gutted the legislature, jailed and exiled rivals, and stole elections. The economic ruin was downstream of a power grab that treated the country as a thing to be owned.
On this view the "socialism vs. sanctions" fight misses the point: the rot was in the seizure of power itself.
▶ without A real revolution, then besieged the sympathetic-left read
Chavista base · parts of the global left
For a decade the misiones genuinely lifted millions out of poverty and gave the excluded a stake — measured by the UN, not invented. Then oil fell and Washington moved to strangle it, and the gains were deliberately erased.
The achievements were real while they lasted; the question is whether anything could have made them last.

Liberation, on this read — the end of a tyranny, not the start of a colony.

the hinge ↕
The petrostate trap

Strip the slogans and both poles are arguing about one machine: sell oil, import everything, save nothing, and buy political loyalty with the proceeds. That machine is older than Chávez — it is what Punto Fijo ran too — and it survives Maduro. High prices hid every flaw; the 2014 crash exposed all of them at once; the gutted PDVSA meant the country couldn't even pump its way out; and then sanctions turned a deep wound into a hemorrhage. "Socialism" and "imperialism" are each describing a blow that landed on a body already made fragile by oil. The real question underneath is whether any government of a single-commodity rentier state could have escaped — or whether the resource was the curse all along.

Tags are rough weight — how much pull a position has in the argument right now — not a verdict. "Traction" here means how many serious people hold it, not who is correct; the two keep separate books.
III  the cruxes

Four fair fights, each carrying part of the load

The positions are bundles of answers; these are the questions that, answered one way or the other, split them. Both panels give the side's best case, in its own voice.

Crux 01 — the timingeconomics · causation

Did socialism wreck the economy, or did sanctions?

The collapse and the blockade both happened. The fight is over the order of events — what was already falling, and what the sanctions then did to it.

▸ the currency died first
It was already in freefall
Hyperinflation, shortages, and double-digit GDP losses were in full motion years before the oil sanctions — while America was still buying the oil.

Put the dates side by side. GDP contracted nearly 5% in 2014. By 2017 the country was in hyperinflation; by 2018 prices rose more than 130,000% in a year. The broad oil sanctions did not arrive until January 2019. For the entire descent into catastrophe, the United States was Venezuela's largest oil customer, dollars were flowing in, and the shelves were still empty — because price controls had made production a losing business, expropriation had scared off investment, and the printing press was covering the deficit. You cannot blame a 2019 blockade for a 2016 famine. The sanctions landed on a corpse and are now credited with the murder.

▸ they slammed the only exit
Sanctions turned crisis into catastrophe
A country can recover from a deep recession; the 2017 financial sanctions blocked the debt restructuring that recovery required, and the 2019 oil sanctions cut off the dollars for food and medicine.

Yes, the economy was sick by 2016 — and a sick economy can still be treated. The standard treatment is to restructure your debt and stabilize the currency. The August 2017 sanctions made that illegal: no U.S. person could touch new Venezuelan bonds, so no restructuring was possible, and the spiral was locked in. Then the 2019 sanctions on PDVSA severed the country from its single largest cash market at the exact moment it needed every dollar for imports. Weisbrot and Sachs tied the squeeze to tens of billions in lost revenue and tens of thousands of excess deaths. Sanctions did not start the fire — they bolted the doors and took the water away. That is the difference between a crisis and a humanitarian catastrophe.

the crux Was the collapse already determined by internal policy, with sanctions a cruel addendum — or did sanctions convert a survivable downturn into the worst humanitarian disaster in the region's modern history? The whole within-vs-without fight rests here.
what would move them

The within side concedes if the economy was stabilizing or recovering when sanctions hit, and the sanctions are what reversed it.

The without side concedes if the descent into hyperinflation and mass shortage was already irreversible before the 2017–19 sanctions arrived.

Crux 02 — the namepolitical economy · crossways

Is "socialism" the right name for what failed?

Before you argue causes, you've already decided what kind of thing collapsed — an economic system, or a state form. Most of the fight is downstream of that.

▸ an ideology did this
Socialism, by its own logic
Abolishing the price mechanism, seizing firms, and printing money to fund the state is socialism in practice, and it fails the same way wherever it's tried.

The defenders always say "that wasn't real socialism" after the fact, but look at what was actually done: price controls on thousands of goods, the expropriation of farms, factories, and stores by the hundreds, a currency regime of multiple fake exchange rates, and a central bank printing bolívars to cover a bottomless deficit. That is not a corruption of socialist policy; it is socialist policy, the suppression of the market in favor of central command. And it produced the result it always produces — the calculation problem made concrete, with empty shelves and a worthless currency. Calling it merely "corruption" or "bad luck" lets the ideology slip the charge, when the ideology is precisely what dismantled the institutions that make an economy run.

▸ a petrostate did this
The oil state, not the ideology
Oil-dependent states collapse across the whole political spectrum; what failed in Venezuela is the rentier petrostate, and "socialism" is a label pasted on after.

If socialism were the cause, you'd expect non-oil socialist economies to detonate the same way and oil capitalism to be safe — but neither holds. The Shah's Iran, Nigeria, Mexico, Russia: oil states of every ideology lurch from boom to bust, hollow out their non-oil industries, and rot from corruption — the textbook "resource curse." Meanwhile Norway runs a vast state oil company and a generous welfare state without any of this, because it saved and diversified. What broke Venezuela wasn't the redistribution; it was a one-commodity economy that saved nothing, a state oil firm gutted for loyalty, and a government that mistook a price spike for permanent wealth. "Socialism" is the name the winners gave it; "petrostate" is the mechanism.

the crux Did an economic system fail — central planning, wherever it appears — or a state form, the oil-rentier petrostate, under bad management? ↳ this crux runs crossways to the others — it's the hidden second axis the within-vs-without line is plotted against.
what would move them

The ideology side concedes if oil states of every ideology collapse alike, and comparable non-oil economies running these policies do not.

The structure side concedes if it's the specific socialist measures — controls, expropriation, monetary financing — that drove the collapse, independent of the oil dependence.

Crux 03 — the gainsdevelopment · what counts as progress

Were the Chávez-era gains real, or a mirage bought with oil?

Poverty did fall by half; illiteracy was declared gone; the barrios got clinics. Whether that was development or a sugar high is the question.

▸ borrowed time, not progress
A consumption boom, not development
The gains were funded by an unrepeatable oil windfall, with nothing saved and the productive economy hollowed — so they evaporated the moment prices fell.

Real development builds capacity that survives a bad year — industries, institutions, savings. Chavismo did the opposite: it spent a once-in-a-generation oil windfall on present consumption, saved none of it, ran down the strategic reserves, expropriated the firms that might have produced something, and left the country importing even its food. So when the price broke, there was nothing underneath. The poverty rate didn't just rise back — it blew past where it started, because the "gains" were never anchored to anything but the barrel. A welfare boom you can only afford while oil is at $100 isn't progress; it's a high-interest loan against a future the government chose not to fund. The eight million who fled are the repayment.

▸ real lives, really better
The gains were measured, and lived
Halved poverty, near-universal literacy, healthcare in neighborhoods that never had it — certified by the UN, lived by millions for a decade. Their later destruction doesn't unmake them.

These weren't propaganda numbers. ECLAC and the UN recorded poverty falling from roughly 49% to the mid-20s, the lowest inequality in Latin America, secondary enrollment jumping, and UNESCO crediting the literacy drive with effectively ending illiteracy. For a decade, people who had been invisible to the state had doctors, schooling, and subsidized food — and dignity, which doesn't show in a GDP table. That a windfall paid for it is true of every oil state's golden age, including the ones the West admires. And that the gains were later destroyed says something about the oil crash and the blockade that destroyed them, not about whether they were real while they lasted. You don't get to point at the wreckage and say the house was never built.

the crux Is welfare funded by a commodity windfall real progress for as long as it lasts — or is durability the test, making impermanent gains a mirage by definition? You can read the very same statistics either way.
what would move them

The mirage side concedes if the social gains were built on a savings-and-investment base that could have outlasted the oil price.

The real-gains side concedes if the improvements were statistical artifacts of the boom that left no lasting change in people's lives.

Crux 04 — the raidsovereignty · the present

Was capturing Maduro liberation, or imperialism?

On 3 January 2026 U.S. forces flew a sitting president to a New York courtroom. Whether that closed a tyranny or opened a colony is the live question.

▸ a tyranny ended
Overdue justice
A narco-indicted dictator who starved his people and stole the 2024 election was removed; the constitution's succession clause was followed, and most Venezuelans wanted him gone.

Maduro wasn't an elected president defending a sovereign mandate; he was a man who lost the 2024 vote — the opposition has the tally sheets — and kept power by force, under a U.S. narcotics indictment, while presiding over the worst peacetime collapse in the hemisphere's history. Sovereignty is not a shield a regime earns by jailing its opposition and faking its elections. When he was taken, Venezuela's own Supreme Tribunal invoked Article 234 and the vice-president succeeded him; the state did not fall into chaos. Within months, hundreds of political prisoners walked free. Ask the eight million who fled, or the diaspora that danced in Doral, whether this was their colonization or their relief. A people does not need permission from the man oppressing them to be glad he's gone.

▸ the empire took the oil
Regime change, dressed as drug war
A foreign military seized the head of a sovereign state atop the world's largest oil reserves — then backed Maduro's own vice-president over the elected opposition. The pattern is old and the tell is the oil.

Strip the moral language and look at the act: the United States bombed and raided another country to abduct its president, with no declaration of war and no UN mandate, and flew him home for trial. Whatever Maduro was, that is the prerogative of empire, not of law — the same prerogative exercised against Mossadegh in 1953, attempted in Caracas in 2002, and rehearsed in Iraq. And watch what followed: not the elected opposition installed, but Maduro's hand-picked vice-president recognized by Washington in exchange for cooperation and oil access negotiated within weeks. If this were about democracy, the people who actually won the 2024 election would be governing. "Drug war" is the wrapper; the largest crude reserves on earth are the contents. A precedent that a superpower may seize any leader it indicts is not a victory for Venezuelans — it's a leash.

the crux Does removing a tyrant most of his people rejected justify a foreign power seizing a head of state — or does the precedent of empire outweigh any one regime's crimes, whatever the man deserved? It's the crux that's about us as much as them. And it may be both at once.
what would move them

The liberation side concedes if the transition installs a pliant client over the opposition that actually won, and locks in U.S. control of the oil.

The imperialism side concedes if the August 2026 elections are free, the 2024 winners take power, and the oil stays Venezuela's.

IV  the payoff

Four cruxes, two crossed questions underneath

One axis is the loud one: was Venezuela ruined from within or strangled from without. But cut sideways and a quieter axis appears — did some actor choose this (Chávez's ideology, or Washington's malice), or did a structure impose it (the oil-rentier trap that boom-and-busts whoever holds the palace)? The socialism-vs-sanctions argument is being plotted against that second question all along. Plot both and the field stops looking like one fight. It looks like people standing on different squares, each answering a question the other never asked.

Crux 01 · the timing
Socialism or sanctions

The horizontal axis, asked of the dates. A collapse already underway, or one the blockade created.

Crux 02 · the name
Ideology or petrostate

The vertical axis — the hidden one. A system that fails everywhere, or an oil state that fails like all oil states.

Crux 03 · the gains
Progress or mirage

The same statistics, read by durability or by the decade they were lived. Where the moral weight of the project sits.

Crux 04 · the raid
Liberation or empire

The horizontal axis brought to the present. A tyranny ended, or a precedent of seizure — possibly both.

Most arguments about Venezuela are really arguments about oil — the vertical axis. Work out whether you think an actor chose the ruin or the barrel imposed it, and you'll usually find your opponent disagreeing with you about that, and only seeming to disagree about socialism and sanctions.
