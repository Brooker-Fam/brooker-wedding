FIELD · metaethics — the status of right and wrong

Is right and wrong discovered — or made up?

Torturing a child for fun is wrong, and it stays wrong even if a whole society cheers it on. Almost everyone feels that. The fight is over what the feeling is: a fact we detect, like the mass of an electron, or a verdict we hand down and then forget we authored. This isn't the question of which acts are right — it's the question of what kind of thing "right" even is. A dozen positions fold into four cruxes, and those into one or two questions.

One axis runs under all of it. Watch the two colors.

I  the lineage

Twenty-four centuries arguing about what "wrong" means

Read top to bottom. A dot's halo marks where the thinker stands — green for moral facts we discover, gold for morality we invent, lilac for the view that the split is a trap.

c. 380 BCE
Platophilosophy · the Forms
The Form of the Good sits at the top of the Republic's ladder, more real than anything you can touch, lighting up everything else the way the sun lights a field. Justice isn't a deal we strike. It's out there, and reason climbs toward it.
c. 350 BCE
Aristotlephilosophy · the good life
Drop the heavenly Forms; keep the realism. In the Nicomachean Ethics, what's good for a human is fixed by what a human is, the way good soil is fixed by what plants need. Eudaimonia — flourishing — is the target, and you can be wrong about it. Realism with its feet on the ground.
1739
David Humephilosophy · is/ought
Buried in the Treatise, the sentence that broke the spell: writers slide from is to ought without ever explaining the jump. Reason can't make that move — it's "the slave of the passions." Survey a murder all you like; the wrongness isn't in the facts, it's in the sentiment you bring. The headwater of every later anti-realism.
1785
Immanuel Kantphilosophy · the Categorical Imperative
The Groundwork finds objectivity without Plato's furniture. Test your reason for acting: could everyone act on it without the whole thing collapsing? Lying fails. The moral law binds any rational agent at all — yet it's spun out of reason itself, not read off the cosmos. The hinge the constructivists will keep returning to.
1903
G. E. Moorephilosophy · the open question
Principia Ethica sets a trap for anyone who'd define "good" as some natural property — pleasure, say, or what we desire. For any such definition, "but is that actually good?" still makes sense, so the definition missed. Goodness is real and simple and non-natural, grasped directly. Naming the "naturalistic fallacy" shaped a century of argument.
1936
A. J. Ayerphilosophy · emotivism
Language, Truth and Logic: "stealing is wrong" states no fact and can't be true or false. It vents disapproval, the way "boo!" does — a feeling with a grammar borrowed from description. The boo-hooray theory, and the first sharp form of the idea that moral talk doesn't describe at all.
1944
C. L. Stevensonphilosophy · persuasion
Ethics and Language gives Ayer's slogan some machinery. Moral words carry "emotive meaning"; using them is a bid to shift how the other person feels and acts, not a report on the world. Calling something good is an attempt to bring you around.
1977
J. L. Mackiephilosophy · error theory
Ethics: Inventing Right and Wrong grants the realist what they mean: ordinary moral talk really does claim objective, built-in wrongness. Then it says every such claim is false. Two charges — the argument from queerness (such facts would be unlike anything else we know) and the argument from relativity (people disagree too deeply for there to be one truth they're all missing).
1988+
The Cornell realistsphilosophy · naturalism
Boyd, Sturgeon, Railton answer Moore on his own ground: moral facts are natural facts — facts about harm, welfare, what lets people thrive — and we find them the way we find any natural kind, by inquiry that pays off. No spooky properties required, just a science of the good.
1993
Simon Blackburnphilosophy · quasi-realism
Essays in Quasi-Realism tries to have it cheap. Start where the emotivist starts — with attitudes, not facts — then earn, step by step, the right to talk like a realist: to say moral claims are true, to argue, to think you can be mistaken. If you can rebuild the whole edifice from feelings, why pay for the facts?
1996–2011
Korsgaard · Street · Parfitphilosophy · the live front
Korsgaard's Sources of Normativity (1996) revives Kant: obligation comes from what a rational agent must will. Sharon Street's 2006 Darwinian Dilemma turns evolution against realism. Parfit's On What Matters (2011) answers with the last great defense of non-natural facts. The argument that won't end, now fought here.
Into the present

The big survey of professional philosophers puts more of them on the realist side than most outsiders guess — a slim plurality, not a settlement. The energy has drained out of the old slogans and into two places: constructivism, which tries to build morality from agency, and the debunking arguments, which ask whether an evolved animal could track moral truth even if there were any.

II  the positions

Who holds what — and where it sits on the axis

Two families of question: whether there are moral facts at all, and what a moral sentence is even doing when you say it. A card's left edge is its lean; the tag is rough sociology, not a scorecard.

Are there moral facts? the metaphysics

◀ discovered Non-naturalist realism a resurgent minority
Moore · Parfit · Shafer-Landau · Enoch
There are real moral facts, and they don't reduce to facts about atoms or brains or what anyone wants. Goodness is its own thing, grasped by reason — irreducible, and none the worse for it.
The standing worry: how does a physical animal come to know facts that float free of the physical world?
SEP: Moral Non-Naturalism
◀ discovered Naturalist realism a strong contemporary camp
Boyd · Sturgeon · Railton
Moral facts are natural facts — about flourishing, suffering, what social creatures need — and you discover them the way you discover anything else, by looking and testing. No second world of moral stuff floating above this one; just this one, morality included.
Moore's open question is the bill it has to keep paying: why doesn't "but is welfare really good?" still feel open?
SEP: Moral Realism
◀ discovered Divine-command ethics central in religious thought
Augustine · Aquinas · Adams
Morality is grounded in God — in his nature, or in what he wills. Right and wrong are as objective as anything, because they rest on the one being who can't be wrong.
Plato's Euthyphro set the trap that won't let go: is it good because God commands it, or commanded because it's good? One horn makes morality arbitrary; the other puts the good above God. (See the God map.)
▶ made Error theory small but serious
Mackie · Joyce · Garner
Ordinary moral talk aims at objective facts — and there are none, so all of it is false. "Slavery is wrong" is in the same boat as "Zeus is angry": a sincere claim about something that isn't there.
The bullet to bite: if every moral claim is false, so is "torturing children is wrong" — which most people take as a reductio, and error theorists take as honesty.
Mackie, Inventing Right and Wrong
▶ made Relativism popular outside philosophy, weaker within
Westermarck · Harman · Wong
Moral truth is real but indexed — true relative to a culture, a framework, a way of life. What's right "for them" can differ from what's right "for us," with no view from nowhere to settle it.
The recurring objection: it seems to bar you from saying a confident, cruel society is simply wrong, which is the one verdict many people most want to keep.
SEP: Moral Relativism

What are moral claims doing? the semantics & psychology

▶ made Expressivism the leading anti-realism
Ayer · Stevenson · Gibbard · Blackburn
A moral sentence expresses an attitude — approval, contempt, a plan — rather than stating a belief that could be true or false. "Cruelty is wrong" airs a stance; it doesn't report a fact about cruelty.
Its hardest puzzle is the Frege-Geach problem: "if lying is wrong, then getting your brother to lie is wrong" embeds the moral bit without expressing any attitude — so what does it mean there?
SEP: Moral Anti-Realism
◆ hinge Constructivism the live middle
Kant · Rawls · Korsgaard · Street
Moral truths are built by a procedure that rational agents can't coherently reject — what you must will to be an agent at all, what principles you'd choose behind a veil of ignorance. Objective enough to bind you, without any fact sitting out there before the procedure runs.
Pressed from both flanks: realists say a built truth is no truth; anti-realists ask why the procedure's outputs count as more than dressed-up preference.
◆ hinge Quasi-realism influential, much debated
Blackburn · (kin: Gibbard)
Concede that morality starts in attitudes — then show why our moral talk so faithfully apes the realist's. We argue, we allow we might be wrong, we say "it's true that cruelty is wrong," and all of it can be earned from feelings, no facts billed.
Critics ask whether, once you've earned every realist sentence, anything is left to disagree with the realist about.
▶ made Moral skepticism & nihilism a persistent undertow
Pyrrho · Nietzsche (contested) · Joyce
Either there are no moral facts to know, or there are but we can't know them. Skepticism doubts our access; nihilism doubts the facts. Both leave the confident moralist owing an account of how he knows.
Lived consistently it's nearly impossible — even the skeptic flinches at gratuitous cruelty — which cuts both ways in the argument.
SEP: Moral Epistemology
the hinge ↕
The view that "discovered vs invented" is a false choice

Constructivism and quasi-realism share one move: deny that you have to pick. Morality is made — yet not made up, not arbitrary, not a coin you could flip the other way. It's built by procedures that hold us the way the rules of logic do. Ask what any rational agent must commit to (Kant, Rawls, Korsgaard) or which attitudes survive scrutiny and reflection (Blackburn's quasi-realism) and you get constraints you can't simply wish away. One wing rebuilds the bindingness from agency; the other rebuilds it from refined feeling. Both tell the realist and the anti-realist the same thing: you can keep the objectivity and skip the metaphysical furniture — nobody had to carve the good into the fabric of the universe for "you ought not torture" to come out true.

Tags are rough sociology — how the literature shakes out — not a verdict. Relativism is a minority view among philosophers and a near-default outside the seminar room; popularity and truth keep separate books.
III  the cruxes

Four fair fights, each carrying part of the load

The positions are bundles of answers; these are the questions that, answered one way or the other, split them. Both panels give the side's best case, in its own voice.

Crux 01 — the factsmetaphysics

Are there moral facts that hold independently of any mind?

Take the surest case you have — torturing an innocent for fun is wrong. Is that a fact you've detected, or a feeling you can't help having?

▸ the intuition is data
Moral realism
That torturing an innocent for fun is wrong is about as certain as anything gets — steadier than the premises of any clever argument out to unseat it.

Run the comparison the skeptic invites. On one side: torturing a child for sport is wrong. On the other: whatever chain of premises concludes it isn't really wrong, only felt to be. Which would you give up first? Nobody who has understood both sentences hesitates. The wrongness is the fixed point; the theory that explains it away is the thing on probation. And drop real moral facts and watch what goes with them — you lose the sense that abolishing slavery was progress rather than a change of fashion, and you lose the standing to tell a confident, cruel culture that it is wrong and not merely different. If those judgments are sound, something makes them sound. That something is a moral fact.

▸ a feeling needn't answer to a fact
Error theory
Grant the certainty — then ask what kind of thing you'd be detecting. A fact that's wrong-in-itself, and pulls you to act just by being seen, would be unlike anything else in the world.

Mackie called it queerness, and meant it as plain description. Everywhere else, facts and motives come apart: the facts sit there, inert, and whether you care is a separate matter about you. A moral fact would have to break that — to be built into reality and automatically action-guiding, gripping any mind that grasped it. Nothing else behaves that way. And what faculty reads it? Not the eyes, not instruments; only "intuition," which is the name for the puzzle, not the solution. The tidier story doesn't posit the strange object at all. The horror you feel at torture is a deep human reaction — selected for, shared, hard to switch off — and it explains everything the realist points to without smuggling in a fact for the feeling to track.

the crux Is the moral intuition evidence of a fact, or a powerful feeling that needn't track one? Pick the first and you're a realist of some stripe; pick the second and the rest of the gold side opens up.
what would move them

The realist side concedes if the apparent objectivity of moral judgment is fully explained by psychology and evolution, with nothing left over that needs real moral facts.

The anti-realist side concedes if moral knowledge behaves like a genuine cognitive achievement — reliable convergence under reflection that attitudes alone can't explain.

Crux 02 — the disagreementanthropology · epistemology

Does deep, lasting moral disagreement show there's nothing to be right about?

Physics converges; ethics doesn't. Honest, informed people have split on the core questions for millennia. What does the split prove?

▸ no convergence, no fact
The argument from relativity
Where there's a fact, inquiry tends to close in on it. Ethics never closes in. The best explanation is that there's no target to converge on.

Look at the track record. On the shape of the planet, the cause of disease, the age of the rocks, disagreement narrows as evidence comes in — that's what having a fact looks like. Morality shows nothing of the kind. Slavery, the treatment of women, what we owe animals, what may be done in war — across cultures and across centuries, thoughtful and informed people land in places that don't reconcile, and time doesn't grind them toward one answer. The simplest reading isn't that everyone but you keeps missing the moral fact. It's that there's no fact there to miss, and what looks like discovery is each society projecting its own commitments onto the world and calling them written.

▸ a hard subject, plus bias
Realism on disagreement
The disagreement is shallower than advertised, much of it isn't even about morality, and every hard field has it without anyone concluding there's no truth.

Start with how much agreement there actually is. Find the culture that prizes cowardice and rewards the murder of its own for sport — you won't. The deep structure is remarkably shared; what varies is the application. And look at where the variation comes from. Much of it traces to non-moral disagreement: whether the enemy tribe are really people, what the gods require, whether the fetus has a mind. Settle those and the moral gap often closes. People who agree the facts but split on the values are rarer than they look. Then there's self-interest, which warps moral judgment the way a thumb on the scale warps a weight — slaveholders found slavery fine, and not by coincidence. Last, the parity point: economists disagree, historians disagree, philosophers of physics disagree, and no one says their subjects are empty. Hard questions breed disagreement. That's a fact about difficulty, not about facts.

the crux Is moral disagreement evidence of no-fact, or just of a hard subject plus bias and self-interest? Both sides see the same data; they read its source differently.
what would move them

The realist side concedes if deep moral disagreement persists even after every non-moral fact is settled and bias is controlled for.

The anti-realist side concedes if most moral disagreement dissolves into disputes about non-moral facts plus shared deeper principles.

Crux 03 — the debunkevolution · epistemology

If evolution shaped our moral sense, can it track moral truth?

Our intuitions came from ancestors who out-reproduced rivals. Selection rewards fitness. Why think it also handed us the truth?

▸ fitness, not truth
Street's Darwinian Dilemma
Selection built the moral sense that helped our ancestors survive — not the one that tracks independent moral facts. The realist owes us a wild coincidence, or a confession.

Why do we feel that we owe more to our own children, that betrayal is vile, that returning a favor is owed? Because the proto-humans who felt those things banded together, raised young, and left descendants — us. Selection was reading fitness, not goodness. Now the realist faces a fork. Say the two have nothing to do with each other, and our moral faculty was aimed at survival, so we've no reason to trust it as a guide to mind-independent moral facts — it would hit them only by fluke. Or claim the faculty does track the truth, and you've asserted a staggering coincidence: that the very intuitions natural selection installed for breeding success just happen to line up with the moral order of the cosmos. Neither fork is comfortable, and there's no third.

▸ then math falls too
Companions in guilt
The same acid eats arithmetic, logic, and perception, which evolved on the same terms — and nobody gives those up. Either selection can favor getting things right, or moral facts are close enough to facts about flourishing that the gap never opens.

Run the dilemma against our grasp of number. Our math sense evolved too, because counting predators and tracking shares paid off — so by the same logic we should distrust arithmetic, which no one does. The honest response is that getting reality right is often exactly what keeps you alive: the animal that correctly judges the gap is jumpable, or that this berry poisoned the last forager, out-survives the one with comforting falsehoods. Truth-tracking can be fitness-enhancing; selection and accuracy aren't strangers. And for the naturalist there's a second reply — if moral facts just are facts about suffering and cooperation and what social animals need, then a faculty tuned by social living to be sensitive to those very things isn't coincidentally tracking the truth. It's tracking the truth by tracking fitness, because here they're the same facts seen twice.

the crux Does an evolutionary origin sever the link between our moral beliefs and any moral truth — and if it does, why doesn't it take math down with it? The realist's escape and the debunker's reach stand or fall together.
what would move them

The realist side concedes if the evolutionary debunking argument is shown to hit moral belief specifically while sparing math, logic, and perception.

The anti-realist side concedes if the debunking argument proves too much, taking down our reliable faculties along with morality.

Crux 04 — the oughtnormativity · crossways

Where do binding "oughts" come from — outside us, or from us?

"You ought not torture" feels like a demand on you whether or not you want to comply. Is that bindingness given to us, or authored by us?

▸ reasons that don't wait on wanting
Categorical reasons
Some oughts bind you whatever you happen to want. "Don't torture for fun" doesn't pause to check your desires — and that unconditional grip is the whole thing a theory of morality has to save.

Tell a man who tortures for pleasure that he has no reason to stop unless he already wants to, and you've said something false. The reason holds against his wants, not in service of them — that's what makes it a moral reason and not a tip about how to get what he's after. This is the datum, the phenomenon any account has to keep: morality presents itself as a set of demands you don't get to opt out of by shrugging. An ought you can dissolve by changing your preferences was never the moral ought to begin with; it was a hypothetical in disguise. Save the bindingness or you've changed the subject.

▸ no ought from an is alone
Humean & constructivist oughts
An "ought" tied to nothing — no desire, no attitude, no agreement — is unintelligible. Hume's gap means you never wring an ought from bare facts. Oughts come from us.

Ask what a free-floating reason, binding regardless of every desire and commitment a person has, would even be. Not a fact about the world — Hume showed you can't squeeze an ought out of an is, and adding more is-facts never closes the gap. So the bindingness has to enter somewhere on our side of the line. The expressivist locates it in the attitudes we express and stand behind: to call torture wrong is to forbid it, with feeling, and to hold others to that. The constructivist locates it in agency itself — you can't act at all without treating some ends as worth pursuing, and pull that thread and you're committed, on pain of contradiction, to principles you can't consistently refuse. Either way the demand is real and it isn't optional. It just doesn't float free. It bottoms out in what valuing, willing creatures are bound to.

the crux Are moral reasons external, categorical givens, or do they bottom out in our attitudes and agreements? ↳ this crux runs crossways — it's the second axis: not whether moral claims are true, but whether oughts are found or authored.
what would move them

The realist side concedes if categorical, desire-independent reasons are shown to be unintelligible — every real “ought” tracing back to some aim or attitude.

The anti-realist side concedes if even one reason is shown to bind regardless of anyone's desires or agreements.

IV  the payoff

Four cruxes, two questions underneath

One axis runs left to right: are moral truths discovered, or made? But the field won't lie flat on a single line, because the cleverest moves play a second question against the first — when you say "torturing is wrong," are you stating a fact that could be true, or voicing an attitude? Cross the two and the map opens up. The error theorist and the realist agree completely on the second question (moral claims are fact-claims) and split hard on the first. The expressivist and the realist agree on neither. That's why constructivism and quasi-realism only make sense in two dimensions: they answer "made" on the left-right axis while keeping much of what the top gives you.

Crux 01 · facts
Is there a fact

The horizontal axis itself. The intuition as evidence of a moral fact, or as a feeling that tracks none.

Crux 02 · disagreement
Why no convergence

The horizontal axis stress-tested by anthropology. No-fact, or a hard subject warped by bias.

Crux 03 · the debunk
Could we know it

The horizontal axis, attacked through our origins. Evolution severs the link to truth, or it doesn't — and math is the test.

Crux 04 · the ought
Where bindingness lives

The vertical axis, near enough. Reasons given from outside, or authored from our attitudes and agency.

The deep question is whether moral truths are discovered or made — and the most interesting people on the map, the constructivists, answer "made, but not by anything as flimsy as you're imagining."
