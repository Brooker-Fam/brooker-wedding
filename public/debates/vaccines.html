FIELD · public health, law & the ethics of consent

Is it my body, my call — or our herd, our rules?

A fight with real bodies on both sides. Measles and polio are not hypothetical; neither is a child injured by a shot, nor a nurse fired for refusing one. A dozen positions on vaccines fold into four disagreements, and those into two questions: when may the group override the person, and what counts as proof good enough to act on. The litigator Aaron Siri — who has spent a decade prying loose documents and petitioning to halt specific vaccines — is one way in. There is no risk-free square on this board. Below, the lineage, the positions and who holds them, and four cruxes, each with the strongest case both sides make.

I  the lineage

Two centuries of the same argument, in one column

Read top to bottom. A dot's halo marks where the moment pulls — green toward the individual and the demand for proof, gold toward the collective and the duty to it, lilac for the bargain that tried to split the difference.

1796
Edward Jennermedicine · smallpox
A Gloucestershire doctor notices that milkmaids who caught cowpox didn't get smallpox, then scratches the pus into an eight-year-old boy and exposes him to the killer. It works. Vaccination begins — and so does the case that one person's arm can protect a town.
1853 / 1867
The Vaccination Acts & Leicesterlaw · resistance
Britain makes infant smallpox vaccination compulsory, with fines and jail for refusers. Leicester answers with marches, an Anti-Vaccination League, and parents who go to prison rather than comply. The first organized argument that the state cannot conscript your child's body — bodily liberty as a line in the sand.
1905
Jacobson v. Massachusettslaw · U.S. Supreme Court
Henning Jacobson refuses Cambridge's smallpox shot and is fined five dollars. The Court sides with the city: liberty isn't absolute, and a community facing an epidemic may require vaccination under its police power. The legal cornerstone every American mandate still rests on.
1955
The Cutter Incidentsafety · polio
Cutter Laboratories ships Salk polio vaccine in which the virus was not fully killed. Roughly 200,000 children get live poliovirus; about 40,000 fall ill, 200 are paralyzed, 10 die. Not a rumor, not a fringe claim — a documented disaster, and the honest root of a distrust that never fully went away.
1982
"DPT: Vaccine Roulette"media · movement
A Washington TV station airs an hour on children said to be harmed by the whole-cell pertussis shot. Barbara Loe Fisher, a parent in the audience's shoes, co-founds the group that becomes the National Vaccine Information Center. The modern parents' movement has its spark and its institution.
1986
National Childhood Vaccine Injury Actlaw · the grand bargain
Lawsuits over DPT injuries had driven makers toward quitting the U.S. market. Congress strikes a deal: a no-fault Vaccine Court pays the injured from a tax on each dose, and manufacturers get broad shelter from ordinary product suits. The hinge the whole modern fight turns on — supply secured, the courtroom door narrowed.
1998
Andrew Wakefieldmedia · the spark that misfired
A Lancet paper links the MMR shot to autism in twelve children. It is later retracted as fraudulent, the data manipulated, and Wakefield struck off the U.K. medical register. The claim was discredited — and it still lit the fuse on a generation of distrust that outran the correction.
2004–2015
The autism studiesepidemiology · replication
A Danish cohort of half a million children, then study after study across countries and decades, find no MMR–autism link. U.S. reviews by the Institute of Medicine and federal agencies back the existing safety record. The mainstream's answer to 1998, delivered in population data rather than anecdote.
2019 / 2021
Hesitancy named, then mandatespublic health · autonomy
The WHO lists "vaccine hesitancy" among its top ten threats to global health. Two years on, COVID-19 vaccine requirements — for jobs, campuses, restaurants — meet the largest pushback in living memory, fought less over efficacy than over coercion and consent.
2020s
Siri, ICAN & the takeoverlaw · the present fight
Aaron Siri and the Informed Consent Action Network win document disclosures through the courts and file petitions to pause or pull specific products. In 2025, Robert F. Kennedy Jr. — long the movement's loudest voice — takes charge of Health and Human Services. The challenge is now inside the building it used to picket.
Into the present

The argument has moved from the street to the agency. What counts as proof, and who gets to decide for a child, are open in a way they haven't been for decades — now contested by the people running the system, not only those outside it.

II  the positions

Who holds what — and where it sits on the axis

Two families of position: one about who gets to decide, the other about what we're allowed to count as proof. A card's left edge is its lean; the tag is rough sociology, not a scorecard.

The ethics & the politics who decides

◀ individual Medical freedom & informed consent vocal, litigious minority
Aaron Siri · ICAN · NVIC
Your body is yours to govern. A medical act you're pressured into isn't consented to, and consent is the value everything else answers to — including, when they collide, public-health goals.
The fight here is about the right to refuse, not a claim that vaccines fail; some on this side vaccinate and still hold the line on choice.
Informed Consent Action Network
◀ individual Religious & philosophical exemption long-standing, varies by state
parents · some faith communities
Even granting that vaccines work, conscience sets a limit the state shouldn't cross; a sincere objection earns an exemption rather than a penalty.
Sidesteps the science question entirely — it's about whose belief governs the child, not whose data is right.
▶ collective Public-health communitarianism institutional mainstream
CDC · AAP · most practicing physicians
Vaccination is partly a civic act. Your choice doesn't stay in your body — it reaches the newborn too young to be vaccinated and the kid on chemo who can't be — so the community has a stake worth weighing against private preference.
Casts the decision as one you make partly on behalf of others, which is exactly what the freedom view disputes.
▶ collective Calibrated mandates · the Jacobson tradition the working legal status quo
most courts · school-entry law
Require a shot only where the disease is both dangerous and contagious enough to justify it — measles for the classroom — and keep exemptions on the books. A scalpel, not a hammer.
Tries to honor both poles at once, which is why critics on each side find it either too soft or too coercive.

The evidence & the system what counts as proof

◀ individual "Show me the saline trials" outside the biomedical consensus
Aaron Siri · RFK Jr.
Test a new vaccine against an inert placebo, the way the rest of medicine does, and run a long-term study comparing the fully vaccinated to the never-vaccinated. Until that exists, the safety case has a hole in it.
Frames the demand as ordinary scientific rigor; the disagreement is over whether that rigor is achievable or already met by other means.
Siri — Senate testimony
▶ collective Evidence-based consensus overwhelming in the literature
the biomedical mainstream
The trials we have, plus surveillance across hundreds of millions of doses, already settle safety and efficacy to the standard medicine uses for everything else. "No inert placebo" doesn't mean "no evidence."
Rests on the idea that population data and active-comparator trials are real proof, not a consolation prize for the trial skeptics want.
Science-Based Medicine
◀ individual Regulatory-capture critics a live worry well beyond the fringe
reform-minded skeptics · some economists
The FDA runs partly on fees from the firms it regulates, staff cycle into industry, and the 1986 liability shield blunts the usual market reason to chase down rare harms. Conclusions from a captured system shouldn't get the benefit of the doubt.
An argument about institutions and incentives, not about any one molecule — which is why it's hard to answer with a single study.
▶ collective Defenders of the 1986 system the prevailing legal-policy view
vaccine-law scholars · public-health agencies
The no-fault Vaccine Court has paid out billions to injured families, often faster and with a lighter burden of proof than a jury would demand, and it kept vaccines on American shelves when liability nearly drove them off.
Treats the shield as the price of a stable supply plus a working compensation route — a trade, openly made, not a cover-up.

the hinge ↕
Trust, but verify — and earn it

A growing camp holds two things at once: the core science is sound, and the system around it has fed an avoidable trust deficit that now powers most of the autonomy war. Their fix isn't to ban or to mandate harder — it's to publish the raw data, run inert-placebo trials wherever doing so is ethical, overhaul a Vaccine Court that injured families find slow and adversarial, and pull mandates back to the diseases that warrant them. Evidence-reform voices in academic medicine — Vinay Prasad and Marty Makary among them, before Makary took over the FDA in 2025 — argue from inside the mainstream that openness about uncertainty is what rebuilds confidence, and that defensiveness is what corrodes it. To both poles they say the same thing: a lot of the fight is over how the science has been handled, not what it shows.

Tags are rough sociology — how the field and the courts shake out today — not a verdict. The skeptic positions are a small share of physicians and a large share of the public conversation; "mainstream" counts noses in the profession, and noses aren't proof.
III  the cruxes

Four fair fights, each carrying part of the load

The positions are bundles of answers; these are the questions that, settled one way or the other, split them. Both panels give the side's best case, in its own voice.

Crux 01 — the proofepidemiology · ethics

How much proof is enough — and can you ethically give one group a saltwater shot?

An inert placebo is the cleanest test in medicine. Once a vaccine works, withholding it from the placebo arm means leaving children exposed on purpose. Both can't fully win.

▸ only an inert placebo is a real test
Show me the saline trial
A saltwater placebo is the gold standard everywhere else. Test a vaccine against an old shot or an adjuvant instead, and a harm both arms share can vanish from the result.

When a drug is tested against a sugar pill, any difference is the drug's doing. Swap in an active comparator — another vaccine, or the aluminum adjuvant alone — and you've lost that. If both arms carry the same risk, the trial reports "no difference" and calls a shared harm invisible. That's not a quibble; it's how you'd hide a signal if you wanted to. And the one study that would answer the deepest worry — track the fully vaccinated against the never-vaccinated over years, for every outcome — has never been run. Sitting on a question that's this cheap to ask, and this easy to answer if the answer is reassuring, is itself a tell. We're not asking for the impossible. We're asking for the rigor you already apply to a blood-pressure pill.

▸ a saline arm can be a death sentence
Equipoise & surveillance
Once you know a vaccine stops measles, giving some children saltwater instead knowingly leaves them open to it. That breaks the ethics of running a trial at all.

A randomized trial is only permitted when you genuinely don't know which arm is better — clinical equipoise. The moment the evidence says a vaccine prevents a dangerous disease, a saline arm isn't neutral; it's assignment to avoidable risk, and no ethics board should approve it. So we use active comparators on purpose, and it's an accepted design, not a dodge. Safety doesn't stop at licensure either: VAERS catches early signals, and the Vaccine Safety Datalink tracks tens of millions of real doses against matched records, which is how rare harms actually surface. As for the never-vaccinated study — families who refuse every shot differ from everyone else in income, health care, and a dozen other ways you can't scrub out, and randomizing children into a no-vaccine arm for years is exactly the experiment ethics forbids. The demand sounds like rigor and resolves into something no honest review board could sign.

the crux Is observational plus active-comparator evidence enough to act on, or is only an inert-placebo RCT real proof? Where you set that bar decides whether the current safety record reads as solid or as full of holes.
what would move them

The individual side concedes if the long-term, transparent safety data they ask for — including the placebo or vaccinated-vs-unvaccinated comparisons — come back clean.

The collective side concedes if a population-scale signal of serious chronic harm turns up that current surveillance missed, or an ethical inert-placebo trial is shown feasible and still refused.

Crux 02 — the mandateethics · law

When, if ever, may the state require a shot?

Contagion means your choice can reach a stranger's lungs. Autonomy means no one else gets to choose what enters your bloodstream. The mandate lives exactly where those two collide.

▸ "comply or lose your job" isn't consent
Bodily autonomy
Genuine informed consent is the floor of medical ethics. Take the shot or lose your paycheck, your seat in class, your access to public life — that's coercion wearing consent's clothes.

The right to refuse a medical procedure is settled ground; we don't make people donate a kidney even to save a life. A vaccine mandate breaches that floor and then dresses the breach as a free choice — agree to be injected, or be shut out of work and school. Pressure that heavy isn't a choice anyone freely makes. Blanket rules compound it: they ignore the person who already had the disease and carries immunity, the one with a real medical risk, the one whose own math comes out differently. Strip away the slogans about the common good and what's left is the state, or your employer standing in for it, claiming a say over what crosses your skin. That's the one thing autonomy was supposed to put off-limits.

▸ your lungs are not only your business
Harm to others
A contagious disease makes vaccination partly a decision about other people — the infant, the transplant patient — and we already curb liberty to keep one person's choices from landing on another.

Autonomy over your own body is real, and it ends where the virus you're carrying reaches someone who had no say. We don't treat drunk driving as a private lifestyle choice; we don't let a restaurant skip the health code because the owner feels fine about the risk. Contagion is the same structure — your decision rides home in someone else's body, often a person who can't be vaccinated and is relying on the rest of us. Jacobson settled in 1905 that a community may require it, and the mandates that followed came almost always with medical and often religious exemptions; this isn't injection at gunpoint. And it works in a way pleading never did: measles and polio didn't retreat because we asked nicely. School requirements closed the gap that voluntary uptake leaves open every time.

the crux How dangerous and how transmissible must a disease be before the collective interest outweighs individual choice? Everyone draws the line somewhere; the fight is over where.
what would move them

The individual side concedes if a disease is contagious and deadly enough, and the vaccine good enough at stopping spread, that opting out plainly endangers specific other people.

The collective side concedes if the vaccine doesn't meaningfully block transmission, or voluntary uptake and exemptions can reach herd protection without coercion.

Crux 03 — the shieldlaw · economics

Does shielding makers from lawsuits protect the supply, or kill the safety incentive?

Mandate a product and bar most suits over it, and you've made an arrangement that exists nowhere else in American commerce. Bargain or hazard?

▸ no other product gets this deal
Liability is the feedback loop
For every other product, the threat of a lawsuit is what punishes unsafe design. "You must take this, and you can't sue if it hurts you" is uniquely hard to justify.

Tort law is the market's safety check. A carmaker that hides a defect gets sued into fixing it; that exposure is why the recall happens. Vaccines are carved out of this. The 1986 Act routes most injury claims away from regular courts and into a federal program, and the Supreme Court later read it to bar even well-grounded design-defect suits. So the one product government compels you to accept is also the one you mostly can't take to a jury. Worse, a maker insulated from that risk has weaker reason to fund the long-term studies skeptics keep asking for — the lawsuit it's dodging is the very thing that would otherwise force the data into the open. Remove the consequence and you've dulled the strongest incentive a company ever has to find its own product's harms first.

▸ the suits nearly ended the supply
No-fault, by design
In the mid-1980s, litigation almost drove vaccine makers out of the U.S. entirely. The 1986 Act is the fix: no-fault compensation, funded by a tax on every dose, that has paid billions to injured families.

This shield wasn't a gift to industry — it was a rescue of supply. DPT lawsuits in the 1980s pushed manufacturers to the exits, and a country with one pertussis-vaccine maker left is a country one bad quarter from running out. So Congress built a different road: you don't have to prove negligence, you petition a dedicated court, and a per-dose excise tax funds the awards. It has paid out billions, often faster and on a gentler standard than a jury demands, to families a normal product suit might have turned away. Regular tort law, with its decade-long fights and all-or-nothing verdicts, is a clumsy safety tool anyway; the real check is the regulator and the surveillance system, not the plaintiff's bar. The shield buys a stable supply and a working compensation route, and it does both in plain sight.

the crux Is liability the indispensable safety incentive, or the necessary price of keeping vaccines on the market? Same statute, read as a hazard or as a bargain.
what would move them

The individual side concedes if the no-fault Vaccine Court is shown to compensate the injured better and faster than lawsuits would, with no drop in safety effort.

The collective side concedes if the liability shield is shown to have dulled safety incentives or starved long-term studies, and lifting it wouldn't collapse the supply.

Crux 04 — the trustepistemology · crossways

Can the agencies and the companies be trusted — and what follows if they can't?

Before scoring the other three fights, ask who you'll believe when the data comes in. This is the question hiding under the rest.

▸ deference has to be earned
Trust is not an argument
User-fees, revolving doors, and a track record that includes Vioxx and the opioid approvals mean "trust the experts" can't be the end of a sentence. Earned, maybe. Assumed, no.

Tell me to defer, and I'll ask what the deference is worth. The FDA collects a large share of its drug-review budget from the companies it judges; its staff move into industry jobs and back; and the agencies that now vouch for vaccine safety are the same institutions that waved through Vioxx and signed off on the opioids that seeded an overdose epidemic. None of that proves any vaccine is unsafe. It does prove that "the experts say so" is an appeal to authority, not evidence, and that an authority with this history owes us its work, not our faith. Show the raw data. Open the trials. Let outsiders check. A system confident in its results doesn't need to be taken on trust, and the reflex to demand trust instead of transparency is itself a reason to withhold it.

▸ vibes are worse than imperfect data
Convergence across rivals
Distrust of institutions is healthy; trading replicated, multi-country evidence for gut feeling is not. And the anti-vaccine economy runs on money too — follow it both ways.

Skepticism toward power is right and proper, and the conflicts are real — police them hard. But notice what the evidence actually does: the same conclusion turns up in Denmark and Japan and the U.S., from academics, governments, and groups with clashing incentives who would love to be the one to overturn it. Capture can bend a single study; it can't easily make independent teams on three continents converge on the same wrong answer. Meanwhile the machine selling doubt — the supplements, the books, the documentaries, the litigation practices — is itself a profit center, and "follow the money" cuts toward it just as sharply. The honest move when you distrust an institution is to check its work against the people who'd most like to prove it wrong. When they keep landing in the same place, that convergence is the signal, imperfect messengers and all.

the crux Does institutional entanglement invalidate the evidence, or is convergent, independently replicated data trustworthy despite imperfect institutions? ↳ this one runs crossways to the other three — it's the hidden second axis, deciding whose answers to 01–03 you'll even credit.
what would move them

The individual side concedes if the core safety and efficacy findings replicate across independent groups with no industry ties and opposing incentives.

The collective side concedes if a specific, consequential finding is shown to have been bent by industry funding or the revolving door — not merely vulnerable to it in theory.

IV  the payoff

Four cruxes, two questions underneath

Cruxes 01 through 03 are three angles on one question: when does the group get to decide for the person — about proof, about mandates, about who eats the risk. Crux 04 runs crossways and asks something prior: when the data lands, will you believe the people who gathered it? Plot both and the shouting resolves into a map. Most people who think they disagree about vaccines turn out to be standing at different heights on the trust axis, answering a question the other side never asked.

Crux 01 · proof
What counts as evidence

Inert-placebo RCT, or surveillance plus active comparators. The horizontal axis, asked of the data.

Crux 02 · mandate
Who decides for whom

The horizontal axis at its sharpest: when contagion lets the group override the person.

Crux 03 · shield
Where the risk lands

The horizontal axis, run through the 1986 law. Lawsuits as safety, or as a threat to supply.

Crux 04 · trust
Whose word to take

The vertical axis. Whether captured institutions can still produce believable data — the question that decides if the other three even land.

Find your own hinge. Few people are moved by all four — one carries the weight. Locate it, work out whether you're really arguing about my body, my call or about whose evidence to believe, and you'll usually find the other person standing on the vertical axis, not the horizontal one.
